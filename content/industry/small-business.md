## What makes a CRM "right" for a small business

The needs of a 5-person team are not a scaled-down version of the needs of a 500-person team. Small businesses don't have a CRM admin, don't run multi-stage approval workflows, and won't pay for unused capabilities. The CRMs in this category are the ones that get out of the way: contact and pipeline management, simple automation, native email and calendar sync — and pricing that doesn't escalate steeply across tiers.

## What to prioritize

- **Time to first value.** If a rep can't log a deal within 15 minutes of signup, the tool will lose to a spreadsheet. The best small-business CRMs ship with sensible defaults and fast import.
- **All-in-one vs best-of-breed.** Tools like Nutshell, EngageBay, and Bigin bundle CRM + email marketing + landing pages so you don't pay for three subscriptions. Best-of-breed (Pipedrive + Mailchimp + Calendly) is more flexible but more setup.
- **Pricing per real seat.** "From $9/user/mo" headlines often hide minimum-seat or annual-only requirements. Check the tier you'll actually need (automation usually lives one tier up).
- **Mobile.** Small-business reps live in the field. A CRM without a working mobile app will be abandoned within a quarter.
- **Email integration.** Two-way Gmail/Outlook sync is the single highest-leverage integration. Without it, reps copy-paste threads and stop logging activity.

## Common mistakes when choosing

Buying for the future you imagine instead of the team you have. Salesforce can handle a 200-person team — it's overkill at 5. The cheapest tier of an enterprise CRM is almost always worse than the top tier of an SMB-focused one at the same price.

The other common mistake is optimizing for features your team won't use in year one. Forecasting, territories, multi-currency, custom roles — these matter eventually, not now. Pick the tool that wins on workflow today and revisit at headcount 25.
