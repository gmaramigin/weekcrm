A general contractor's business is won long before the first crew shows up. It's won in the pursuit: the invitation to bid, the site walk, the takeoff, the proposal, and the weeks of follow-up while the owner or GC decides. That pursuit is a sales pipeline, and most contractors run it out of a truck cab — a stack of business cards, a phone full of missed calls, and an estimating tool that knows how to price a job but nothing about whether you're going to get it. Estimating software like Buildertrend, Procore, or STACK is excellent at takeoffs, schedules, and change orders once you've won. None of them manage the hunt for the next contract.

That's the gap a CRM fills for a GC. It tracks every bid you're chasing, who invited you, when the proposal went out, and when it's time to call before someone underbids you. Just as importantly, it manages the relationships that actually feed the pipeline — the architects, developers, property managers, and past clients who send you the good work. A CRM doesn't price the job or schedule the trades. It makes sure the phone keeps ringing and no proposal dies in a follow-up gap.

## How we picked

We weighted what matters for a contracting business built on bids and referrals: clean capture of bid invitations and inbound leads from your website, phone, and email; a pipeline that can hold a dozen active pursuits with real dollar values and close dates; automated follow-up so a proposal never goes cold while you're on a site; reporting that shows your bid-to-win ratio and which referral sources actually convert; and a price that makes sense for a shop running a handful of jobs at a time. We also weighted mobile, because a GC is in the field, not at a desk. None of these replace estimating or project management — assume the CRM handles the pursuit and hands the won job off to your ops tools.

## What to consider

- **Best for contractors marketing for work → HubSpot.** If you're running a website, chasing SEO, and want inbound bid requests captured and attributed to source, HubSpot's forms, landing pages, and free tier pull leads into one place and its automation nurtures the slow ones. Strongest choice if marketing drives your pipeline.
- **Best for a clean bid pipeline you'll actually use → Pipedrive.** Pipedrive's visual pipeline maps perfectly onto a GC's stages — invited, walked, proposed, negotiating, won — with dollar values and reminders on every deal. For a contractor who wants clarity without complexity, it's the fastest to live with from the cab.
- **Best for value as the shop grows → Zoho CRM.** Managing more pursuits and adding a estimator or two, Zoho gives you pipeline, workflow automation, and reporting at a price that holds, plus the wider Zoho suite if you want forms and email under one bill.
- **Best for automated follow-up and referral nurture → Keap.** Keap is built for the follow-up that wins jobs: an instant reply to a bid request, a sequence that keeps a proposal warm, and a "we appreciate your business" cadence to past clients and referral partners. If your win rate lives and dies on follow-up, its automation and SMS are the tool.
- **Best for contractors who run ops on boards → monday.com.** If your team already tracks jobs, punch lists, and subs on monday boards, monday CRM adds a bid pipeline in the same workspace, so pursuit, project handoff, and operations share one home.

## What a general-contractor CRM should track in 2026

1. **Active bids and their dollar value.** Every pursuit with the contract value, the decision date, and the stage — so you know your real pipeline, not a guess.
2. **Who invited you and why.** The GC, developer, or owner behind each bid, tracked as a relationship, so you know which sources send work worth chasing.
3. **Proposal follow-up.** When the estimate went out and when it's time to call — the single most common place a winnable job quietly dies.
4. **Referral and partner relationships.** Architects, engineers, property managers, and past clients who refer you, maintained so the relationship stays warm between projects.
5. **Bid-to-win ratio by source.** Which invitation sources and job types you actually win, so you stop chasing bids you never close.
6. **Won-job handoff.** When a contract signs, the clean handoff into estimating and project management so nothing falls between sales and ops.

## When this category is the right call

A CRM earns its keep the moment you're chasing more bids than you can hold in your head. A one-crew contractor working entirely off word-of-mouth referrals from a single builder may not need one yet. But once you're fielding bid invitations from multiple sources, sending several proposals a week, and depending on a network of referral partners to keep the pipeline full, the pursuit is too important to run out of a phone. The trigger isn't revenue — it's the number of open pursuits and relationships you're trying to keep warm. When following up on proposals starts falling through the cracks, the CRM is the fix.

## Pricing snapshot

Realistic 2026 entry pricing (per month, billed annually):

- **HubSpot** — Sales Hub Starter around $20/seat; free tier for basic forms and contacts.
- **Pipedrive** — Essential around $14/seat; cleanest low-cost bid pipeline.
- **Zoho CRM** — Standard around $14/seat; strong value as the crew and pipeline grow.
- **Keap** — from around $249/month, bundling contacts and users; automation and SMS are the core.
- **monday.com** — CRM plans roughly $12–$28/seat depending on tier, usually with a small seat minimum.

Prices and promotions shift — confirm current rates before you commit.

## Trial advice

Load your real open bids into the trial on day one — every proposal you're currently chasing, with its dollar value and decision date. That's the honest test of whether the pipeline view actually helps you see the money in motion. Then wire up one follow-up automation: a reminder to call three days after a proposal goes out. Log a week of genuine bid invitations and referral calls as they come in. At the end, ask whether you could see your total pipeline value at a glance, whether the follow-up reminders would have caught a job you'd otherwise have let slip, and whether it was fast enough to update from your phone between site visits. If it survives a real week in the field, it'll survive the year.
