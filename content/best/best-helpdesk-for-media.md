  - q: "Why do newsrooms resist ticketing systems?"
    a: "Because journalists correctly perceive that a tips inbox is not a ticket queue. A source email needs discretion, a fast personal reply, and often internal discussion before anyone responds — not a case number and an auto-acknowledgment. **Missive** and **Front** succeed here because they keep the email feel while adding assignment, internal comments, and shared drafting.   - q: "How do you handle subscription and paywall issues efficiently?"

  - q: "Should corrections requests go through the helpdesk?"
    a: "They should arrive there and route out immediately. Corrections are an editorial standards process with its own reviewers, documentation requirements, and legal implications. The helpdesk's job is to capture the request with the article URL and the specific claim disputed, acknowledge it, route it to the standards editor, and track that it got a response. It should never be resolved by a support agent using a macro."

## How we picked

Media organizations have a support problem that splits cleanly in two, and most buying mistakes come from trying to solve both halves with one shape of tool. Half is subscriber operations: billing, paywall access, app login, cancellations. That is high-volume, repetitive, and highly automatable — classic helpdesk territory. The other half is relationship correspondence: tips, pitches, corrections, rights and permissions, ad partnerships. That is low-volume, high-stakes, and destroyed by ticket-shaped process.

So we weighted **dual-mode capability**. We favored tools that can run a structured subscriber queue with SLAs and automation while also hosting an editorial inbox that still feels like email, with internal comments and collaborative drafting instead of case numbers and auto-replies. Front and Missive are the clearest expressions of this; Help Scout sits pleasantly in the middle.

We also weighted **subscription system context** and **cost structure**. Publishers do not have generous support budgets, and headcount in this industry is under permanent pressure. Per-seat pricing that assumes a SaaS company's margins is a poor fit, which is why Crisp's flat-rate workspace model and Hiver's Google Workspace layer both earn places here for smaller publications that need to cover the work without adding a support team.

## What to prioritize

- **Subscription platform integration.** Plan, renewal date, payment status, entitlements, and last login inside the conversation, whether you run Piano, Zuora, Stripe, Recurly, or an in-house system. Without it, every billing ticket is a manual lookup and your agents become human database queries.
- **Self-service cancellation.** Make it genuinely easy. Forcing readers to email to cancel inflates your support volume, guarantees a hostile interaction, and in the US and EU increasingly attracts regulatory attention. The support savings alone justify building it.
- **An editorial inbox that does not feel like a helpdesk.** No case numbers on tips, no auto-acknowledgment on a source email, no macro on a corrections request. Missive's collaborative drafting and Front's comment threads let editors work the way they already do while keeping assignment and accountability.
- **Corrections routed, never resolved, by support.** Capture the article URL and the disputed claim, acknowledge receipt, hand to the standards editor, and track the outcome. This is a documented editorial process with legal exposure and support should be the intake, not the decision.
- **AI deflection on access problems.** "I'm subscribed but I'm hitting the paywall" is the most common and most solvable media support ticket. An AI agent with access to the entitlement record can walk the reader through the browser, account, and device causes without a human touching it.
- **Pricing that survives a lean support team.** Most publications support tens of thousands of subscribers with a handful of people. Evaluate per-seat cost against that reality — Crisp's flat workspace price and Hiver's Google Workspace model exist for exactly this constraint, and Zendesk only makes sense once subscriber volume genuinely justifies it.
