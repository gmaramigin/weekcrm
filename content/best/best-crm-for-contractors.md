## How we picked

Contractors live in two worlds the average sales CRM ignores: the bid and the build. A quote turns into a job, the job generates change orders, and every step has to reconcile with accounting at the end. We weighted CRMs that bridge that gap — estimate-to-invoice continuity, mobile access for crews standing in a half-framed kitchen, and follow-up automation for the bids that go quiet for a week. Pure pipeline tools that stop at "deal won" scored lower than ones that carry a project through to a paid invoice.

## What to consider

- **Run everything off QuickBooks** → Method CRM. Native two-way sync means an estimate built in Method posts straight to QuickBooks and payments flow back without duplicate entry.
- **Solo or two-person trade business** → Thryv. Booking, automated review requests, and a customer portal in one subscription, so you skip stitching three apps together.
- **Growing crew that wants room to scale** → Zoho CRM. The cheapest path from a free tier to multi-stage pipelines, territory rules, and Zoho Books invoicing.
- **Chasing a lot of competitive bids** → Pipedrive. The drag-and-drop board makes it obvious which estimates are stalling and which need a same-day call.
- **Appointment-driven service contractors** → vcita. Online booking, deposits, and SMS reminders aimed at HVAC, electrical, and repair work where the calendar is the business.

## Pricing snapshot

Zoho CRM is free for three users and roughly $14/user/month on Standard. vcita starts near $29/month for a solo operator with booking. Pipedrive runs $14–$49/user/month depending on automation depth. Thryv is quote-based and tends to land around $200/month for the full marketing-plus-CRM bundle. Method CRM sits at $25–$49/user/month, with the higher tier unlocking the full QuickBooks field set most contractors actually need.

## Trial advice

Test with a real bid, not a demo contact. Build an estimate, push it to your accounting tool, mark the job won, and generate the invoice — that round trip is where contractor CRMs either save hours or create double-entry headaches. If your crew works off phones, install the mobile app first and confirm a tech can update a job status and snap a photo from a job site with no Wi-Fi. Whichever tool survives that workflow with the fewest manual steps is the one to buy.
