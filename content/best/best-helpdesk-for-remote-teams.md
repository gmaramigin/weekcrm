faq:
  - q: "What breaks first when a support team goes remote?"
    a: "Tacit knowledge transfer. In an office, a new agent overhears twenty resolutions a day and absorbs the exceptions nobody documented — which customers get special treatment, when to escalate, which policy is enforced loosely. Remote, that channel disappears entirely, and it does not get replaced by a wiki. The fix is making the conversation itself the training material: mandatory internal notes explaining why an answer was given, a searchable archive, and a saved-reply library that encodes the exceptions rather than leaving them in someone's head."
  - q: "How do you handle a follow-the-sun handoff cleanly?"
    a: "With a structured handoff note on every open conversation, not a shift-change message in a channel. The receiving region needs three things per ticket: current status, what has been promised and by when, and what the next action is. Tools with per-conversation internal comments make this natural; tools where notes live in a separate chat make it evaporate. Also configure business hours per team so SLAs pause correctly across regions — otherwise your APAC team inherits breached tickets they never had a chance to answer."
  - q: "Is a shared inbox enough, or do we need real ticketing?"
    a: "Under about eight agents with mostly email volume, a well-run shared inbox is genuinely enough and is faster to adopt. You need real ticketing when three things appear: SLAs someone is accountable for, multiple channels that must reconcile to one customer, and reporting that a manager will be measured on. ## How we picked

Remote support teams fail for a reason that has almost nothing to do with software features: the informal knowledge network that holds a co-located team together does not survive distribution. In an office, an agent stuck on an unusual refund request turns around and asks. Remote, that same agent either guesses, waits an hour for a Slack reply, or escalates something that did not need escalating. So we ranked these tools primarily on how well they replace hallway conversation with something durable and searchable, rather than on ticket-handling depth.

The second criterion was async-first design. A distributed team spread across three time zones spends most of its working hours without overlap, which makes every real-time dependency a bottleneck. Tools that assume a colleague is available now — @-mentions with no context, chat-based escalation, approval flows that block — degrade badly. Tools built around leaving a complete written state on the record so the next person can pick it up cold degrade gracefully. That distinction is why Front and Missive rank above products with longer feature lists.

Third, we weighted the number of places an agent has to check. Every additional surface — inbox, ticket queue, chat, project tool, wiki — is a place work goes to die when nobody is sitting next to you to notice. The best remote setup is the one with the fewest tabs, which is why the right answer for a Slack-centric company is genuinely different from the right answer for a Gmail-centric one. Buy the tool that consolidates into where your team already is.

## What to prioritize

- **Internal comments attached to the conversation, not a separate channel.** Every discussion about a ticket should live on the ticket permanently. This is the mechanism that turns your archive into onboarding material and makes handoffs possible without a meeting.
- **Draft review and shared composing.** The ability for a colleague or lead to see and edit a reply before it sends is the highest-value remote feature there is — it replaces over-the-shoulder review for new hires and de-risks sensitive replies. Front and Missive do this best; Missive uniquely supports genuine real-time co-authoring.
- **Collision detection.** Two agents in different time zones answering the same ticket is a weekly occurrence on distributed teams and it is embarrassing every time. Confirm the tool shows who is viewing and typing, and that it does so reliably rather than after a delay.
- **Per-team business hours and SLA pausing.** Your SLA clock should respect the coverage of the team that owns the ticket. Without this, the region that starts its day inherits breaches and the metrics become meaningless.
- **A searchable archive of past resolutions.** Distributed teams cannot rely on institutional memory, so search quality is a training tool, not a convenience. Test it with a vague query a new hire would actually type, not with an exact keyword.
- **Coverage reporting by hour of day.** You need to see contact volume mapped against your actual staffed hours across regions. Almost every remote team discovers a gap they were not aware of, and no amount of individual productivity fixes it.

## Where each one fits

Front is the strongest overall fit and it is not close on the collaboration dimension. Internal comments, shared drafts, assignment across teams, and a conversation model that makes handoffs explicit are exactly the primitives a distributed team needs, and they cover the internal-communication gap that most helpdesks ignore. At $25 per user on Starter, $65 on Professional, and $105 on Enterprise it is not cheap, and the AI tools cost extra, but the Professional tier is where most distributed teams end up and it earns the price.

Missive is the value pick and quietly excellent. It unifies email, SMS, WhatsApp, and chat in a team inbox where colleagues co-author replies in real time — closer to a shared document than a ticket queue. At $18 per user per month with a free plan available, it is the best cost-per-capability option here for a small distributed team, and its 4.6 rating is the highest of any product on this list. The trade-off is thinner formal ticketing and reporting than Front or Freshservice offer.

Unthread solves a specific and increasingly common situation: your remote company runs on Slack, requests arrive in Slack channels, and nobody wants a separate portal. It tracks and resolves tickets from Slack channels natively at $50 per agent per month, with AI handling the repetitive tier. It is the right answer for internal support at a Slack-first remote company, and the wrong one if your customers are external and email-based.

Hiver, Help Scout, and Freshservice cover the remaining shapes. Hiver at $25 per user is the pick for a remote team standardised on Google Workspace — shared inboxes, assignment, and SLAs layered on top of Gmail, so agents learn nothing new. Help Scout at $25 per user with a free tier is the clean, simple choice for a small async team handling external customers, with an interface that stays out of the way. Freshservice is the different animal here: if your problem is internal IT supporting distributed employees rather than external customer support, its asset management, self-service portal, and service catalogue are built for exactly that, from $19 per agent on Starter with the useful automation at Growth.

## Write the handoff protocol before you buy the tool

The most common mistake distributed support teams make is expecting software to solve a process gap. Before evaluating anything, write down what a handoff note must contain, how an agent escalates when nobody is online, and what the maximum acceptable time is for an internal question to go unanswered. Then evaluate tools against that document. Teams that do this pick faster and adopt more successfully, because they are shopping for a specific behaviour rather than a general improvement. Teams that skip it buy the tool with the best demo and rediscover the same handoff problem eight weeks later with a larger invoice.
