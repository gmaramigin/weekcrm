## How we picked

Relationship building is the opposite of pipeline pushing. Success isn't a closed deal this quarter — it's a network that stays warm over years. We prioritized CRMs that reduce the friction of staying in touch: automatic context capture from email and calendar so you never start from a blank record, recency signals that show who's drifting, and gentle reminders to reconnect before a relationship goes cold. We discounted heavyweight sales tools whose deal-centric design makes simply tracking a person feel like overhead. Lightweight, human-feeling interfaces won, because a relationship CRM you dread opening is one you'll abandon.

## What to consider

- **Warm, people-first day-to-day use** → Folk. Contact-centric design, one-click enrichment, and a feel closer to a smart address book than a sales database.
- **Relationships that need to scale into a full CRM** → Attio. Start relationship-led, then add structure, automation, and reporting without switching tools.
- **Relationship selling with social context** → Nimble. Pulls social profiles and shared history into the record so every outreach is informed.
- **AI that tells you who to reconnect with** → Cloze. Reads your inbox and meeting history to flag at-risk relationships automatically.
- **Deliberate personal keep-in-touch practice** → Dex. Set a cadence per person and let it remind you — purpose-built for individuals tending a network.

## Pricing snapshot

Dex runs about $12/month for individuals. Cloze sits around $17–$26/user/month depending on tier. Folk starts near $20/user/month and is the sweet spot for small relationship-led teams. Nimble is roughly $25/user/month. Attio is free to start and scales to $29–$119/seat, making it the only pick here that grows from a personal habit into an organization-wide system without a migration.

## Trial advice

Import your real contacts and connect your actual email and calendar on day one — these tools are worthless on a fake dataset, because their value is the context they pull from your own history. Then look at what the CRM tells you unprompted: does it surface a relationship you've neglected, fill in a contact's recent role change, or remind you to follow up on a thread you forgot? If a week in it's quietly resurfacing people you should reach out to, it's working. If you have to feed it everything manually, keep looking.
