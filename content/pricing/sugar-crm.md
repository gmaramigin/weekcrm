## The seat floor is the real price

Every published Sugar Sell tier requires fifteen users. That single constraint
does more to determine what you pay than the choice between Standard, Advanced
and Premier, and it is the first thing to check before you get attached to the
$59 figure.

If you have eight salespeople, you are still buying fifteen seats. That is
$885 a month for eight working logins — an effective $110 per person actually
using the system. The list price only becomes honest once your headcount
crosses the floor. This is deliberate: Sugar has spent the last decade walking
away from the small-business market it originally served as an open-source
project, and the minimum is the gate.

## What fifteen, twenty-five and fifty seats actually cost

Annual commitment, list price, no discount:

| Seats | Standard ($59) | Advanced ($85) | Premier ($135) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 15 (minimum) | $10,620/yr | $15,300/yr | $24,300/yr |
| 25 | $17,700/yr | $25,500/yr | $40,500/yr |
| 50 | $35,400/yr | $51,000/yr | $81,000/yr |

Those are software-only numbers. They do not include implementation, data
migration, or the Sugar Serve and Sugar Market subscriptions if you need
service or marketing alongside sales.

## Where the tier jump earns its money

Standard to Advanced is $26 per user per month — at fifty seats, $15,600 a
year. That is worth paying when your revenue model is complicated: multiple
product lines with different forecast treatments, territory or split-credit
rules, or automation that has to reach outside the CRM through the API. It is
not worth paying if what you actually want is a tidy pipeline with named
stages and a weekly report. Plenty of teams buy Advanced for two features and
use neither of them by month four.

Advanced to Premier is a support and governance decision rather than a feature
one. You are buying the top response tier, higher platform ceilings, and the
administrative controls a compliance team asks for. If nobody in your
organization has ever filed a P1 ticket at 2am, Premier is not for you.

## Annual only, and what that means

There is no published monthly option. You sign for twelve months up front,
which changes how you should evaluate the product. With a self-serve CRM you
can buy first and decide later; with Sugar you have to decide first. Use the
sales-led demo properly, get your own data into a sandbox, and pressure-test
the customization story before signing — because the usual "we'll cancel next
month" fallback does not exist here.

Discounting on multi-year terms is normal in this segment, and Sugar's pricing
varies by region and contract size. If you are buying more than twenty-five
seats, the list price is an opening position rather than a final one.

## Who this suits and who it punishes

Sugar's pricing works for a twenty-five to two-hundred-person mid-market
company with a non-standard sales process and an in-house admin who will use
Studio. The per-seat rate is defensible against Salesforce Enterprise, the
on-premises option is genuinely rare at this price point, and the
customization depth means you are less likely to buy three bolt-on tools later.

It punishes anyone under fifteen people, anyone who wants to start small and
grow into the product, and anyone who needs sales plus service plus marketing
on a tight budget — that combination becomes three separate contracts. Teams
in that position tend to end up with Vtiger CRM or HubSpot instead, both of
which let you start at one seat.

## A note on accuracy

Sugar publishes these figures on its own pricing page and adjusts them by
region and deal size. Anything quoted here is list price as of July 2026 —
check the vendor's page before you build a budget on it, and assume the number
on your actual quote will differ once volume and term are negotiated.
