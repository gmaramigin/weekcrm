## The meter is conversations, and the definition matters

Almost every competitor in this category bills per agent. Tidio bills per
billable conversation, which it defines as any live chat, ticket, email or
social thread that a human agent replies to at least once. Unanswered visitor
messages do not count. Threads that Flows or the Lyro AI agent close without
human involvement do not count. And a conversation counts exactly once
regardless of how long it runs.

That definition is the whole pricing model. Your bill is not a function of team
size — it is a function of how many customer questions your humans had to
touch. Two support people handling 1,800 conversations pay more than eight
people handling 300.

## Automation that actually lowers the invoice

Because AI-resolved conversations are not billable, improving Lyro's resolution
rate directly cuts your cost per month. This is unusual and worth stating
plainly: on most platforms, AI is an add-on line item that increases the bill.
On Tidio it is the lever that reduces it.

The catch is the allowance. Free and Starter each include 50 Lyro AI
conversations as a one-time grant, not a monthly refresh. So the tier where
saving money via AI matters most is the tier where you have almost no AI to
spend. Meaningful Lyro volume starts at Growth, where the allowance is quoted
per account, and Premium exists specifically for teams running 3,000+ AI
conversations with a guaranteed 50% resolution rate.

## Modelling a real month

Say a store handles 900 total inbound conversations in a month. What you pay
depends entirely on the split:

| Lyro resolution rate | Human-touched (billable) | Where that lands you |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0% | 900 | Growth, upper end of its 2,000 range |
| 30% | 630 | Growth, mid range |
| 60% | 360 | Growth, low end |
| 0%, but only 90 inbound | 90 | Starter at $24.17 |

The same store, same traffic, sits at very different points on Growth's price
range depending on how well the AI is configured. That makes Lyro tuning a
finance activity, not just a support one — and it is why the Premium tier's
managed service exists at all.

## Where the tier boundaries actually sit

Free to Starter is a small step: $24.17 a month doubles you from 50 to 100
billable conversations. Take it the moment you exceed 50, because there is no
reason to ration.

Starter to Growth is the meaningful decision. Growth starts at $49.17 and
stretches to 2,000 conversations, so it is a band rather than a price. If you
are doing 150 conversations a month you will be at the bottom of that band; at
1,800 you will be near the top. Ask for the quote at your actual volume rather
than assuming the entry figure.

Growth to Plus is a different kind of purchase. At $300 a month plus usage, you
are buying discounted per-unit rates and a dedicated success manager. It makes
sense when your volume is high enough that a lower rate per conversation
outweighs the base fee, and it is money thrown away below that crossover.

## Who wins and who loses under this model

Tidio is priced well for ecommerce stores with seasonal traffic and lean teams
— you can staff ten people on a Starter plan and pay for the conversations
they actually handle. It is also the right shape for anyone genuinely committed
to AI deflection, since the savings land on the invoice.

It is a poor fit for B2B teams with long, low-volume, high-touch relationships,
where one account might generate forty conversations a year and none of them
are automatable. Those teams get better value from per-seat billing —
LiveChat's flat $49 per agent, or Crisp's flat workspace fee — because their
cost is predictable and their volume is not.

It is also unpredictable at the top. Plus quotes a base plus usage, so unlike a
per-seat plan you cannot know December's bill in November.
