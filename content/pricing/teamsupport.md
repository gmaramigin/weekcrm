## The base price is not the price

TeamSupport's tier table is unusually clean — $45, $65, $85, all per agent, all
billed annually. What makes the actual quote diverge from that table is a
services and add-on menu that most competitors either bundle or hide.

Live chat is the big one. Messaging and live chat costs $15 per agent per month
and is not included on any tier, not even the $85 Scale plan. Any support
organisation that answers customers in real time should read TeamSupport's
pricing as $60, $80 and $100. Compared like that against Zendesk or Freshdesk —
where chat arrives somewhere in the middle of the lineup — TeamSupport sits
clearly at the premium end, and the case for it has to rest on the B2B account
model rather than on cost.

## An onboarding fee that gets cheaper as you spend more

The services pricing runs backwards from the way software is normally sold.
Basic training costs $2,000 on Starter, $1,500 on Professional and $1,000 on
Scale. Professional services bill at $250 an hour on Starter, $200 on
Professional and $175 on Scale.

So the smallest customer, capped at five agents, pays the highest onboarding fee
and the highest hourly rate. A five-agent Starter account is $2,700 in licence
for year one and a $2,000 training bill on top — a 74% uplift on the software in
year one, and it lands before you have resolved a single ticket.

That maths is worth putting in front of a rep. It is also a genuine argument for
skipping Starter entirely if you are anywhere near five agents.

## First-year cost at three team sizes

Assuming live chat for every agent and one round of basic training:

| Agents | Plan | Licence | Chat add-on | Training | Year one |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | Starter | $2,700 | $900 | $2,000 | $5,600 |
| 20 | Professional | $15,600 | $3,600 | $1,500 | $20,700 |
| 50 | Scale | $51,000 | $9,000 | $1,000 | $61,000 |

Two observations. At five agents the non-licence costs are more than half the
total, which is why very small B2B teams should either commit properly or look
elsewhere. At fifty agents the licence dominates and the services fees become
noise — but $61,000 before Insights or extra Playbooks is real money for a
customer support tool, and the Customer Distress Index needs to be preventing
churn worth considerably more than that.

## Where the tier jumps earn out

**Starter to Professional** is not really a feature decision. It is the removal
of the five-user cap, plus deeper customer intelligence. Twenty dollars per
agent per month is a fair price for that, and the lower training fee claws back
$500 of it immediately. Any team that plans to grow should start here.

**Professional to Scale** is the one to interrogate. You are buying three
Playbooks instead of one, access to the Insights module, and cheaper services
rates. If you will not use Insights — which is $270 a month on Scale, so it is
access rather than inclusion — the upgrade is thin. Fifty agents moving from
Professional to Scale costs $12,000 a year in extra licence for benefits that
may total a few thousand.

## Annual terms, and what is not published

Every price TeamSupport shows is billed annually. No monthly option is
advertised and no free trial is listed on the pricing page. This is a sales-led
product: you will get a demo, a scoping conversation and a quote, in that order.

Because of that, several things you would normally verify yourself are not
published — monthly rates, whether Starter's five-user cap is hard or soft, and
what a multi-year commitment buys. Ask about all three explicitly, because a
vendor that negotiates every deal negotiates every deal.

## Who this model suits

TeamSupport prices for B2B software companies with fifteen to fifty support
agents serving accounts rather than individuals, where a single churned customer
is worth more than the annual contract. In that shape, account-level ticket
organisation and distress scoring justify the premium over a general-purpose
help desk, and the services fees round to nothing.

It punishes small teams — the Starter tier is priced and fee-loaded in a way
that makes it the worst value in the lineup. It punishes any B2C or
high-transaction-volume operation, where per-agent pricing at $60 all-in with
chat buys you a product designed for a customer shape you do not have. And it
punishes teams that expected chat in the box.

TeamSupport revises its packaging and services menu periodically, and the
add-on list is longer than most. Their own pricing page is the authority, and
the quote you receive will be the real answer.
