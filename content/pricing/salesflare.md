## What you are really paying for: automatic data entry, metered by seat

Salesflare's pricing has no contact limits, no record caps and no storage tiers. It is a straight per-seat product, which means the only question that moves your bill is how many people need the CRM open. That is refreshingly legible next to vendors that meter contacts, and it reflects what Salesflare sells: automatic capture of emails, meetings, contacts and files from each rep's mailbox. The value is per person because the data source is per person.

The consequence is that Salesflare gets expensive at scale rather than at volume. A two-person consultancy with 50,000 contacts pays $58 a month on Growth. A twenty-person sales team with 5,000 contacts pays $580. If your database is large and your team is small, this pricing model is on your side. If you have a big floor of reps, look hard at whether every one of them needs a seat.

## Growth to Pro is the decision. Pro to Enterprise usually is not.

The $29-to-$49 step buys workflows, user permissions, custom dashboards and — the part people underestimate — a lead credit allowance that goes from 5 a month to 100. Five credits is enough to see how the feature works and nothing more. Any team that prospects will exhaust it in an afternoon and spend the rest of the month wishing they had Pro. Add role-based permissions, which any team above about four people eventually needs so that reps cannot see each other's pipelines, and Pro is where most real deployments land.

Enterprise at $99 is a different kind of purchase. The software is the same as Pro; what you are buying is people — full onboarding, custom training, a dedicated account manager — plus 250 lead credits. That is worth paying for if you are migrating a team off an incumbent CRM with messy data and need someone accountable for the transition. It is not worth paying for if you just want more credits, and the five-user minimum means you cannot dabble.

The middle column is the honest picture of what Salesflare costs a working sales team: roughly $5,900 a year for ten reps with automation and permissions. That is well under enterprise CRM territory and above the cheapest SMB tools — which is exactly where Salesflare positions itself.

## Annual versus monthly, and why the gap matters here

Most CRMs shave 15 to 20% for annual commitment. Salesflare's discount is steeper: $10 a seat on Growth, $15 on Pro, $25 on Enterprise, which works out to 25 to 34% off the monthly rate. Ten seats on Pro costs $7,680 a year monthly versus $5,880 annually — a $1,800 difference.

That gap is large enough to change the decision. The 30-day trial is unusually generous and needs no card, so use it properly: run the whole team on real deals for a month, confirm the mailbox capture works with your mail setup, then commit annually. Cancellation is immediate with no notice period, so the annual term is the main commitment you are making.

## The teams this pricing suits, and the ones it does not

Salesflare is priced for lean B2B teams of roughly three to twenty-five people whose main CRM problem is adoption rather than capability — agencies, SaaS companies, consultancies where the last CRM died because nobody updated it. For that buyer, $49 a seat to have the CRM populate itself is straightforward arithmetic against the hours reps currently spend logging activity.

It is a poor fit for two groups. Very large sales floors will find per-seat pricing with no volume break adds up faster than a platform deal, and organisations that want a free tier for occasional users, managers or finance have no such option — everyone who logs in is a full-price seat. Teams not on Gmail or Outlook should stop before the price conversation entirely, since the automatic capture that justifies the cost depends on those mailboxes.

Prices move and plan contents get reshuffled. Everything above was checked against Salesflare's own pricing page in July 2026; treat that page as the authority before you buy.
