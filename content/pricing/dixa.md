## All channels in the base tier

Dixa's pricing is high by help desk standards — €71.20 to €143.20 per agent per month on annual terms — and the reason is that it bundles what competitors unbundle. Phone, email, chat and social are all in Growth, the entry tier, along with IVR, call recording, callback, voicemail and SLAs.

That matters because voice is where most help desk comparisons break down. Zendesk and Freshdesk both start cheaper and then sell telephony as a separate module; by the time a team running a real phone queue has assembled equivalent capability, the gap narrows sharply or disappears.

So the honest way to compare Dixa is not headline against headline. It is fully-configured-Dixa against fully-configured-competitor, with voice on both sides. Teams that do not run a phone channel will find Dixa expensive; teams that do often find it competitive.

## The AI model is unusual and worth understanding

Mim, Dixa's AI agent, is billed at a flat €0.35 per conversation. No per-seat fee. No monthly allowance. No cap, and no penalty rate once you cross one.

This is the cleanest AI pricing in the category, and it is genuinely different from Intercom's Fin (per resolution, higher rate) or the credit-allowance models used by Attio, NetHunt and Salesmate. You cannot be surprised by an overage because there is no threshold to cross.

You can, however, be surprised by the total.

At real deflection volumes, AI becomes the dominant line on the invoice. That is not a hidden cost — it is disclosed and linear — but it is a fundamentally different budget shape from a fixed per-seat subscription, and finance teams should model it as usage, not software.

The offsetting logic is straightforward: if Mim resolves conversations that would otherwise consume agent time, €0.35 against a loaded agent-minute cost is favourable arithmetic. The risk case is partial resolution — conversations the AI handles and then escalates, where you pay both the €0.35 and the agent time.

## Where the tiers divide

**Growth** at €89 is a complete omnichannel contact centre: every channel, knowledge base, intelligent routing, IVR, recording, callback, SLAs. For most mid-sized service teams this is genuinely enough.

**Ultimate** at €139 adds operational sophistication — routing that pulls external data, skills-based routing, knowledge-centred service, standard AI intent detection, advanced automations, a sandbox, and third-party AI integrations. Teams with complex routing rules or a real KCS practice need this.

**Prime** at €179 is the enterprise tier: SSO, advanced intent detection, advanced insights, custom roles, multiple organisations, queue and claim restrictions, higher API limits.

The awkward part is SSO. It sits only on Prime, which means an organisation with a mandatory single sign-on policy is buying the top tier for an authentication feature. At a €40 per agent premium over Ultimate, that is expensive governance — and it is the item most worth pushing on in a negotiation.

## The add-on layer

Several capabilities sit outside the tier structure entirely, priced per seat on top: AI Co-Pilot, Quality Assurance, AI Auto QA, Advanced Insights, voice transcription, and seasonal agents for peak periods.

Seasonal agents deserve a note. For retail and travel businesses with sharp peaks, the ability to add capacity temporarily rather than carrying annual seats is a real cost advantage — one of the few pricing mechanics in this category built for a business that is not the same size every month.

## Who should buy it

Mid-market and enterprise service teams running genuine omnichannel operations, especially with a significant voice component. Dixa's inclusion of telephony at the base tier and its flat AI pricing suit teams that want predictable channel coverage and transparent automation costs.

It fits poorly for small teams — the sales-led onboarding and per-agent rates make it heavy for a three-person support desk — and for chat-only businesses, where cheaper competitors deliver the same outcome.
