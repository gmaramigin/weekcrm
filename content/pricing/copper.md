## Seats are the only lever, and that makes the maths simple

Copper does not meter contacts, emails sent, or records stored the way HubSpot or Keap do. There is exactly one variable on the invoice: how many people have a login. That is genuinely refreshing — you can grow your database without a pricing conversation — but it also means there is no cheap way to give someone partial access. Copper has no light-agent or view-only seat. A finance lead who opens Copper twice a month to check closed-won totals costs the same $59 as the rep working the pipeline daily.

For a Google Workspace shop this usually still nets out fine, because Copper replaces the spreadsheet-plus-inbox-plus-notes stack rather than sitting on top of it. The trap is seat sprawl during onboarding, when someone decides "everyone should have visibility" and you add twelve non-sales logins at Professional rates.

## The Basic-to-Professional jump is not optional

Copper publishes three tiers and points out on its own pricing page that 63% of customers start on Professional. That statistic is doing a lot of work. Basic gives you Gmail sync, contacts and one pipeline. It does not give you workflow automation, multiple pipelines, or the integration surface most teams assume is table stakes. If your process is "log the email, move the card, follow up manually," Basic is honest value at $23. If you want Copper to nudge a rep after five days of silence, you are on Professional.

The Professional-to-Business jump is a different story. Business at $99 buys higher automation limits, lead scoring and more advanced permissions. That is a real upgrade for a 30-person sales org with several teams and overlapping territories. For a ten-person agency it is $480 a month of headroom you will not touch. Stay on Professional and revisit when you actually hit an automation ceiling, not because Business is the column with the most checkmarks.

## What it costs at real team sizes

Annual billing, USD per month:

| Seats | Basic | Professional | Business |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | $115 | $295 | $495 |
| 20 | $460 | $1,180 | $1,980 |
| 50 | $1,150 | $2,950 | $4,950 |

At 50 seats, Professional runs $35,400 a year. That is the point where you should be negotiating rather than paying list, and also the point where a Google-native CRM starts competing with Salesforce and HubSpot on total cost rather than on convenience.

## Annual versus monthly

Copper discounts up to 26% for annual commitment, and the discount widens as you go up the stack: $6 per seat on Basic, $10 on Professional, $35 on Business. That Business gap is unusually aggressive and is worth reading as a signal — Copper wants larger accounts locked in. If you are piloting with a handful of reps, take the monthly rate for a quarter and eat the premium; the flexibility is worth more than $10 a seat while you are still deciding whether the Gmail sidebar model fits how your team works.

## Who this pricing punishes

Teams that are split across Google and Microsoft. Teams with a lot of occasional users — support, finance, delivery — who need to read CRM data but never touch a deal. And any team hoping to run a large marketing database cheaply, because Copper's per-seat model gives you no discount for being contact-heavy and light on users, but its marketing tooling is thin enough that you will end up paying for Mailchimp or similar alongside it.

It suits small to mid-size B2B sales teams, agencies and consultancies of roughly five to forty people who are all-in on Google Workspace and whose bottleneck is follow-up discipline rather than reporting sophistication. For that profile, $59 a seat for something reps will actually open — because it is already in their inbox — beats a cheaper CRM nobody logs into.

## A note on accuracy

Copper's list prices were verified against copper.com/pricing in July 2026 and exclude tax. Vendors reprice, run promotions, and negotiate annual contracts below list. Treat Copper's own pricing page as the authority before you sign anything.
