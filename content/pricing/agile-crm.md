## Contacts set the tier, automation caps set the frustration

Agile CRM's ladder is drawn in contact volume — 1,000 free, 10,000 on Starter,
50,000 on Regular, unlimited on Enterprise. On paper that is generous: 10,000
contacts for $8.99 a seat is one of the lowest capacity-per-dollar rates in the
all-in-one category, cheaper per contact than EngageBay or Zoho CRM at
equivalent tiers.

In practice the binding constraint for most teams is not contacts. It is the
automation and workflow allowances layered on top. The free plan gives you one
automation rule and one marketing workflow. Starter gives you a handful.
Regular roughly doubles that. If your reason for buying an all-in-one CRM is to
stop doing follow-up by hand, you will hit the workflow ceiling long before the
contact ceiling, and workflow ceilings are only relieved by moving up a full
tier.

There is a third meter running underneath both: **email sends**. Agile CRM
includes an allowance and bills overflow per message at fractions of a cent.
That is cheap per unit and easy to ignore, right up until you send a 40,000-
contact campaign twice a month.

## Free to Starter is the easy call. Starter to Regular is not.

Going from Free to Starter costs $8.99 a seat and buys ten times the contacts,
two-way email sync, email tracking and several more workflows. For any team
doing outbound at all, that is the least controversial upgrade in this
directory. Do it as soon as the free plan's single automation rule starts to
chafe.

Starter to Regular is a different conversation. It is a 3.3x price increase.
Justify it on two things only: you have crossed 10,000 contacts, or you want
telephony inside the CRM so calls log themselves. If neither applies, the extra
workflows are not worth $21 per user per month — you can achieve more with a
$20/month automation tool wired into Starter's integrations.

Enterprise at $47.99 is mostly about governance: access controls, unlimited
automation, automated voicemails, unlimited contacts. That is a plausible
package for a 20-person sales floor. It is not a plausible package for a
five-person team who just want better reporting, because reporting is not what
improves at this tier.

## The billing-term problem

This is the part where Agile CRM is less transparent than its competitors. The
$8.99 / $29.99 / $47.99 rates it promotes are tied to its longest commitment
term. Shorter terms cost more, and different third-party trackers quote
materially different monthly and annual figures.

We could not read Agile CRM's own pricing page directly — the site returns a
403 to automated requests — so the tier prices above are cross-checked against
Capterra and independent published reviews, all of which agree on the three
headline numbers. We have deliberately not printed a monthly rate, because the
sources disagree and a wrong number is worse than no number.

The practical advice: get the term in writing before you pay. Ask specifically
what the rate is at 12 months versus 24, and what happens to it at renewal.

## Who it fits

It fits a budget-constrained team under about 25 people who want sales,
marketing email and light ticketing in one login and are genuinely more
sensitive to price than to polish. At $8.99 a seat with 10,000 contacts, very
little competes on raw cost.

It punishes two groups. Anyone who needs to evaluate a paid feature before
buying — with no trial on paid tiers, you are committing on faith. And anyone
whose business depends on email deliverability at volume, where Agile CRM has a
documented weak record and a metered send model that makes testing at scale
cost real money.
