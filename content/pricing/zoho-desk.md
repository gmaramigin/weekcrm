## Agents are the meter, but tiers are the decision

Zoho Desk bills per agent per month and does not charge by ticket volume, contact count or resolution. A team of eight handling 400 tickets a week pays the same as a team of eight handling 4,000. For a growing support function that is the right shape — your cost tracks headcount, which is the thing you can actually control, rather than demand, which you cannot.

What that means in practice is that the tier you pick matters far more than any usage projection. Standard to Enterprise is a 2.9× multiplier on the same number of people. Getting that choice right is worth more than any negotiation you will do on volume.

## The free tier is real, and it is a trap for exactly one reason

Three agents, forever, with email ticketing, a knowledge base and a customer-facing help center. Most "free" help desks are 14-day trials with a different label. This one is not, and a two-person support team at a small SaaS company can run on it indefinitely without embarrassment.

The reason to be careful: the Free plan has no SLA management and no automation to speak of. Teams stay on it past the point where they should have moved, then discover their response times are drifting with no system telling them. If tickets are being missed rather than merely queued, that is your signal to move to Standard — the $14 buys you SLAs and dashboards, which is to say it buys you the ability to notice problems.

## Where the tier jumps earn their money

**Express to Standard** is the least interesting decision because Express is deliberately narrow — one department, email-first. If you need social channels, community, or product-based ticketing, you are on Standard. Most teams should treat Express as a stepping stone that they will skip.

**Standard to Professional** hinges on organisational shape, not features. What you are buying is multi-department: separate queues, separate agents, separate routing rules. If you support two products or have a billing team distinct from a technical team, Professional is correct and the $9 per agent is trivially justified. If you are one queue, one team, Professional's telephony and time tracking are nice but optional.

**Professional to Enterprise** is the expensive one and it is essentially a chat purchase. $17 more per agent per month, primarily for live chat, custom functions, multi-brand help centers and the deeper AI layer. Enterprise also includes 50 light-agent licences free, which on Professional you would be paying for individually — for a company where engineering and product regularly comment on tickets, that allocation alone can close most of the gap.

A 20-agent team on Standard runs $3,360 a year. The same team on Zendesk's comparable tier is several times that. This is the whole case for Zoho Desk and it holds up well right through the mid-market.

## Annual, monthly and the renewal question

Zoho publishes its plan prices on annual terms and advertises savings of up to 34% for committing. Month-to-month is available and costs materially more — independent pricing trackers put Standard nearer $20 and Enterprise nearer $50 per agent on monthly terms. Given the low absolute numbers, annual is almost always the right call here: at $14 versus roughly $20, a five-agent team saves about $360 a year for a commitment that costs $840.

The thing to press on is renewal. Buyer reviews repeatedly flag unexpected increases at renewal after promotional or first-year pricing lapses. Ask for the year-two rate in writing.

## Who should and should not buy this

The obvious fit is any team already running Zoho CRM or the wider Zoho One suite, where the two-way sync means agents see sales context without a middleware layer. The second fit is cost-constrained SMB and mid-market support teams who need multi-channel ticketing and automation and cannot justify Zendesk or Intercom pricing.

The misfit is a team whose CRM and business systems live elsewhere and who need live chat as a core channel. In that scenario you are paying Enterprise rates while collecting none of the ecosystem benefit, and the comparison against Freshdesk or Help Scout gets much closer. Also be honest about interface density — Zoho Desk gives you a lot of settings, and small teams sometimes find the configuration surface heavier than the problem warrants.
