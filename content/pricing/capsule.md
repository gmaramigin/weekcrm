## The number that actually moves your Capsule bill

Most CRMs make you count seats. Capsule makes you count contacts. Seats do set the headline — $18, $36 or $54 per person per month — but the reason accounts move up a tier is almost always the contact ceiling: 250 on Free, 30,000 on Starter, 60,000 on Growth, 120,000 on Advanced.

That is an unusual shape. A two-person agency with a scraped list of 40,000 prospects pays more than a ten-person team with 20,000 carefully-kept records, because the first one gets pushed to Growth and the second stays on Starter. If you are evaluating Capsule, do the contact audit before the seat count. Import a realistic export of your existing database and see which ceiling you land under — that single number decides which of three prices you pay.

## Starter to Growth: usually yes. Growth to Advanced: usually no.

The $18-to-$36 jump is where Capsule stops being a contact list and starts being a CRM. Growth adds workflow automations, multiple sales pipelines, multiple project boards, team access controls, and the AI enrichment and summary features. If you run more than one sales motion — new business and renewals, say — or you want tasks generated automatically when a deal moves stage, you need Growth. Doubling the price to get automation is steep on paper, but Starter's manual-only workflow becomes a tax on your team's time quickly.

The $36-to-$54 jump is far weaker. Advanced buys 120,000 contacts instead of 60,000, and raises the pipeline and project board caps to 50 each. Those are volume limits, not capabilities. Unless you are genuinely brushing the 60,000-contact wall, paying 50% more per seat for headroom you are not using is money left on the table. Buy Advanced when a limit stops you, not in anticipation.

Two things stand out. First, Capsule stays cheap at small headcount in a way that mid-market CRMs do not — five seats on Growth is $180 a month with automation included, which undercuts most comparable tiers elsewhere. Second, it scales linearly with no volume break published, so at 50 seats the arithmetic starts to look like everyone else's. Capsule's value is sharpest between roughly two and twenty-five users.

## Billing terms and the annual question

Capsule quotes annual pricing by default and advertises up to 14% off versus paying monthly. That is a modest discount by CRM standards — some vendors push 20% or more — which makes monthly billing a defensible choice while you are still deciding. There is no seat minimum and no multi-year contract, so the flexibility is worth more than the saving in your first few months. Commit annually once the tool has survived a quarter of real use.

Adding a third user is the moment the free plan ends. Capsule caps Free at two users, so the third person converts the whole account, not just the new seat: three users on Starter is $54 a month, not $18.

## Who this suits, and who should look elsewhere

Capsule is priced for small businesses that want a tidy contact-and-pipeline system without a platform's worth of modules. Consultancies, agencies, trades businesses and small B2B sales teams get the best of it, particularly on Growth where the automations live.

It punishes two groups. High-volume list owners get pushed up tiers by database size rather than by anything they use — if you hold hundreds of thousands of marketing contacts, a CRM that meters on records is the wrong shape. And teams that need email marketing in the same bill will find Capsule's marketing capability sitting outside the plan entirely, as a Transpond add-on from $11 a month that grows with your list. Compare that stacked total against an all-in-one before deciding Capsule is the cheaper option.

List prices change, plan contents get reshuffled, and regional pricing varies. Everything here was checked against Capsule's own pricing page in July 2026 — treat that page as the authority before you sign anything.
