## Two deployments, identical software

Issuetrak sells the same product two ways. Cloud is $75 per agent per month, billed annually with a three-agent minimum. On-premises is $1,091 per agent as a one-time licence, plus $218 per agent per year for maintenance.

The feature set is identical. Over a hundred features, no tiers, no paywalls on the core: workflows, SLAs, reporting, knowledge management, white-labelling.

And on both, unlimited end users are free.

## Free end users is the structural advantage

Only agents are licensed. Everyone who opens, comments on or follows a request — every employee in an internal deployment, every customer in an external one — costs nothing.

For an internal service desk that is the number that matters. A ten-agent desk supporting three thousand employees pays for ten agents. Competitors that meter end users or gate portal capability by user count produce a very different quote at that shape.

It also means the cost is predictable through growth. Hiring five hundred people does not change the help desk bill.

## The break-even is around year three

The on-premises licence is often presented as obviously cheaper. The maintenance fee complicates that.

| Year | Cloud (per agent, cumulative) | On-premises (per agent, cumulative) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | $900 | $1,309 |
| 2 | $1,800 | $1,527 |
| 3 | $2,700 | $1,745 |
| 5 | $4,500 | $2,181 |

On licence costs alone, on-premises overtakes cloud early in year two and saves about $682 per agent per year thereafter. Over five years on a ten-agent deployment that is roughly $23,000.

Then subtract your own infrastructure: servers, database licensing, backups, patching, TLS, and the administrator time to run all of it. Realistically that pushes break-even to around year three.

Which means on-premises is right for organisations choosing it for control and data residency, on a long horizon, with existing infrastructure. It is not a quick saving.

## SSO is not in the base price

The "no paywalls" claim is accurate about the core product and needs one important qualification: SSO lives in the optional Identity Management module, quoted separately.

For most organisations above about fifty employees, SSO is mandatory. So the published $75 is a floor rather than a price, and any comparison against a competitor that bundles SSO is incomplete until Identity Management is quoted.

The same applies to asset management — essential for IT support, absent from the base — and to chat, billing, surveys and webforms.

Get the whole configuration quoted at once. A $75 base plus four modules is a different product from a $75 base.

## The three-agent minimum

Cloud entry is $2,700 a year. On-premises entry is $3,273 plus $654 annual maintenance.

Neither is a barrier for the mid-market organisations Issuetrak targets, but it does rule out the two-person help desk that a $19-a-seat competitor would serve.

## Where $75 sits

Against pure help desks — BoldDesk at roughly $10 to $20 an agent, HelpDesk.com at $19 to $79, osTicket Cloud at $11 — Issuetrak is expensive.

Against ITSM and service management platforms with comparable workflow depth, SLA machinery, asset capability and white-labelling, it is mid-range and the flat feature set is a genuine advantage. Competitors routinely put white-labelling and advanced workflow in enterprise tiers at $99 or more.

The deciding question is which category you are actually buying in. If you want a shared inbox with tickets, $75 is a large premium. If you want configurable workflows with SLA enforcement, asset tracking and a branded portal for unlimited end users, it is fair.

## What is not here

No AI deflection layer of the kind now standard in the customer-support half of this market. Issuetrak is a workflow and issue-tracking platform, not an autonomous resolution engine.

For internal service desks — its natural home — that absence matters far less than it would for consumer support, because internal tickets rarely deflect well and workflow correctness matters more than volume reduction.

## Who should buy it

Internal service desks and support operations of three agents and up that need configurable workflows, SLA enforcement and a branded portal for a large unlicensed end-user population. The free end users and flat feature set make the cost predictable in a way tiered competitors are not.

It suits badly small teams wanting cheap ticketing, organisations that will discover SSO and asset management are separately quoted after budgeting $75, and anyone treating the on-premises licence as a one-time cost without modelling maintenance.
