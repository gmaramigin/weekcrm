## Two prices per seat

Salesmate's published ladder is clean — $23, $39, $63, custom — and it describes only half of what you pay. Every tier also grants a monthly allowance of automation credits, and credits are what workflows, sequences and AI actions spend. Basic gives 5,000 per user per month, Pro 10,000, Business 15,000.

For a team using Salesmate as a pipeline and an inbox, the credit line never comes up. For a team using it the way the marketing suggests — automated sequences, AI-assisted follow-up, workflow-driven routing — credits become the meter that actually governs the bill, and top-up packs become a recurring line item.

The practical test is simple. Count the automated touches your process fires per contact per month, multiply by your contact volume, and compare against 5,000 per seat. If the answer is close, you are a Pro or Business customer regardless of which features you wanted.

## What each tier unlocks

**Basic** at $23 is a complete small-team CRM: contacts, companies, activities, a deal pipeline, email sync with templates, a meeting scheduler, web forms and custom fields. Nothing about it is a demo. The honest limit is that it has no sequences — outbound cadence, the thing most teams buy a sales CRM for, is not here.

**Pro** at $39 is where Salesmate becomes a sales system. Sequences, product and quote management, ticket management and a team inbox, custom dashboards with goal tracking, formula fields, SSO, and Sandy AI. This is the tier the product is really designed around, and the one most buyers land on.

**Business** at $63 is for teams with structural needs: custom modules to model objects Salesmate does not ship, deal credit splitting for shared commission, SLAs, duplicate management rules, and the power dialer with voicemail drop. The dialer is the common trigger — outbound teams discover it is not in Pro.

**Enterprise** adds governance and service: audit logs, IP restriction, dedicated IP, a named account manager, quarterly reviews and 24/7 support, with onboarding starting at $1,999.

## What the ladder costs in practice

| Team size | Basic (annual) | Pro (annual) | Business (annual) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3 users | $55/mo | $94/mo | $151/mo |
| 10 users | $184/mo | $312/mo | $504/mo |
| 25 users | $460/mo | $780/mo | $1,260/mo |

The Basic-to-Business spread is 174%. That is a wide ladder for a product whose tiers are only three steps apart, and it means tier selection matters more here than at vendors with flatter pricing. Getting it wrong by one step on a 25-seat team is $320 to $480 a month.

## The telephony question

Salesmate is frequently shortlisted because it advertises a built-in phone system. That framing is accurate but incomplete. The calling and texting capability is an add-on with its own charges on every tier, and the features that make high-volume dialling practical — power dialer, voicemail drop — are gated to Business.

So an outbound team's actual cost is $63/user for the tier, plus telephony usage, plus any extra automation credits their sequences consume. Against a stack of a cheaper CRM and a dedicated dialler, Salesmate may still win on integration, but it rarely wins on headline price once assembled honestly.

## Where it earns its money

Salesmate's argument is coverage per dollar. At $39 Pro, you get sequences, quotes, a shared team inbox, ticketing and an AI assistant in one product — a feature set that at HubSpot or Salesforce requires multiple hubs or editions. For a 10 to 30 person sales team that wants one system instead of four, that consolidation is real and the price is fair.

It suits teams that want structure without an implementation project. It suits worse the very small team that only needs a pipeline — Basic's lack of sequences makes it feel thin against free tiers elsewhere — and the enterprise buyer, where the onboarding fee and per-seat rate together start to look like mid-market pricing without mid-market depth.
