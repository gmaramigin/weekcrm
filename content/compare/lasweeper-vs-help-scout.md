## TL;DR

- **Pick Lansweeper** if you need an accurate, continuously updated inventory of every device, license, and vulnerability across your infrastructure.
- **Pick Help Scout** if you need a simple, human-feeling shared inbox to handle customer support conversations at scale.

## Adjacent departments, unrelated jobs

Worth being direct: these two products have almost nothing in common beyond appearing in IT budget lines.

**Lansweeper** is an IT asset management platform. It scans your network **agentlessly** — no software installed per machine — and builds a live inventory of workstations, servers, network devices, printers, cloud assets, and IoT. Its buyers are IT managers, sysadmins, and security teams at mid-market and enterprise organizations who need to answer audit, license, and vulnerability questions with real data.

**Help Scout** is a customer support platform built on a deliberate philosophy: support should feel like a personal email exchange, not a ticket queue. Shared inboxes, an embeddable widget called **Beacon**, a knowledge base, in-app messaging, and an AI assistant. Its buyers are SMB support leads in SaaS, ecommerce, healthcare, and education who found Zendesk too complex and too expensive.

If you're comparing these, the useful first step is deciding which question you're trying to answer — asset visibility, or customer conversations. That resolves it faster than any feature table.

## The internal-IT-desk edge case

There is one scenario where the comparison isn't purely academic: a small IT team looking for both asset visibility and a way to field internal requests.

Help Scout can handle inbound internal requests — it's a shared inbox, and internal tickets are still email. But it's explicitly **not purpose-built for ITSM or internal service desks**. No asset management, no change management, none of the ITIL scaffolding. And Lansweeper has no request-handling capability at all: no queue, no agent workspace, no SLAs.

So even in the edge case, the answer isn't "pick one." It's Lansweeper for the estate, plus something for the requests — and Help Scout is a reasonable lightweight choice for that if your internal needs are simple.

## Simplicity means different things here

Both products lean on ease of use, but the claim lands differently.

**Help Scout's** simplicity is genuine and immediate. The inbox-style interface is familiar to anyone who uses email, cutting onboarding from days to hours. That's a real competitive position against Zendesk, and the **4.5 rating** and free plan for up to 5 users make it one of the easiest evaluations in the help desk category.

**Lansweeper's** ease is on the operational side — agentless scanning means no per-machine deployment project, which is a substantial saving. But **initial configuration and scan tuning require technical expertise** to get accurate results. It's easy to run and non-trivial to set up correctly, which is roughly the inverse of Help Scout's profile.

## Pricing predictability

**Help Scout** publishes everything: free up to 5 users, paid from **$25/user/mo**, Plus at **$45/user/mo** where advanced workflow automation and deeper integrations live. Per-user pricing climbs with headcount, but you can model it exactly.

**Lansweeper** has a **free tier for small environments** — good for a real evaluation — but enterprise pricing is **custom-quoted with no self-serve path**. You can prove the value before you negotiate, which is a decent structure, but you can't budget from the website.

## Who should pick what

- **You need license compliance and audit-ready asset records** → Lansweeper.
- **You're a small support team that wants a warm, email-like inbox** → Help Scout.
- **Security team tracking unpatched and outdated systems** → Lansweeper.
- **You want AI to resolve routine customer requests automatically** → Help Scout.
- **You need a live map of on-prem, cloud, and hybrid infrastructure** → Lansweeper.
- **You found Zendesk too complex and too expensive** → Help Scout.

## Bottom line

There's no meaningful head-to-head here, and pretending otherwise would waste your time. Lansweeper is a strong choice for IT teams that need always-current asset visibility without manual inventory work — particularly if compliance or security audits are driving the requirement — with the caveat that getting scans tuned properly takes technical skill. Help Scout is among the best-liked help desks on the market for small and mid-sized customer-facing teams, and its free tier makes it a near-zero-risk evaluation. Decide which problem is actually in front of you; the tool follows from that immediately.
