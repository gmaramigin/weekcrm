## TL;DR

- **Pick Folk** if your work is relationships — fundraising, partnerships, recruiting, agency BD — and you want a fast, elegant network database with one-click LinkedIn capture.
- **Pick Zoho CRM** if you run a structured sales pipeline and want deep automation, AI, and multi-pipeline management at a fraction of Salesforce's price.

## A network tool vs a pipeline tool

Both are strong CRMs, but they answer different questions. Folk asks: how do I keep track of the people I work through? Zoho asks: how do I move deals through a process efficiently? That distinction drives everything else.

Folk is relationship-led. It pulls contacts from Gmail, LinkedIn, and spreadsheets into a unified book you can tag, filter, and reach out from. Its users — founders, VCs, recruiters, agencies — think in people and introductions, not stages and quotas. The design reflects that: light, fast, and free of the pipeline machinery those users don't want.

Zoho is pipeline-led and proudly feature-dense. It gives you multi-pipeline deal tracking, Blueprint to standardize and enforce sales processes, Zia AI for deal predictions and lead scoring, and a place in the 50-plus-app Zoho ecosystem. Its users want Salesforce-style capability without Salesforce-style pricing, and they're willing to configure to get it.

Neither is objectively better. The mismatch to avoid is forcing relationship work into Zoho's pipeline structure, or trying to run a real sales operation on Folk's intentionally minimal deal features.

## Pricing

Zoho is one of the cheapest full-featured CRMs available: free for up to 3 users, then $14 (Standard), $23 (Professional), $40 (Enterprise), and $52/user/month (Ultimate), billed annually. Folk runs $20 (Standard), $40 (Premium), and $80/user/month (Custom), with a free plan and a 14-day trial.

Zoho is cheaper per seat at every comparable tier and its free plan is more generous. But cost per seat isn't the whole calculation — Folk's premium buys speed on relationship capture that a network-driven team may value more than the dollar difference. For a cost-sensitive team running a conventional pipeline, Zoho is the clear value pick.

## Depth vs restraint

This is the real trade-off. Zoho's strength is that it does almost everything a sales team could ask for, and its weakness is that all that capability makes setup feel complex and the interface heavier than modern rivals. Folk's strength is restraint — it does relationship management beautifully and refuses to do the rest — and its weakness is that if you actually need pipelines, forecasting, or deep reporting, it can't stretch to meet you.

Note that Zoho gates its best automation and Zia AI to the Enterprise and Ultimate tiers, so unlocking the depth that distinguishes it from Folk means paying $40+/user rather than the $14 headline. Folk's capabilities, by contrast, are more evenly available across its simpler plan structure.

## Adoption risk

For a small non-sales team — a founder plus a couple of colleagues doing partnerships — adoption is the real risk, and Folk is engineered to win it: modern UI, simple onboarding, immediate value. Zoho asks more of you up front, which pays off for teams that need its depth but can stall a lightweight team that just wanted to stop losing contacts in a spreadsheet.

## Who should pick what

- **Founder or VC managing relationships and dealflow** → Folk.
- **Recruiter or agency working a network of people** → Folk.
- **SMB sales team running structured pipelines on a budget** → Zoho CRM.
- **Team already using Zoho Mail, Books, or Desk** → Zoho CRM.
- **Non-technical team that needs to be productive today** → Folk.
- **Team that needs multi-pipeline forecasting and process automation** → Zoho CRM.
