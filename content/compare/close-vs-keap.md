## TL;DR

- **Pick Close** if you have a dedicated sales team that needs to make high call volumes, run email and SMS sequences, and track pipeline with minimal CRM admin. Close is one of the best outbound sales tools in the market.
- **Pick Keap** if you're a small business that needs to automate the entire client journey — capture, nurture, book, invoice — without separate tools for CRM, email marketing, and payments.

## Pricing

Close and Keap have different pricing structures that make direct comparison tricky. Close is per-user, per-month: around $49/user/mo at Startup, $99/user/mo at Professional, and $139/user/mo at Enterprise. Those prices include built-in calling at higher tiers. Keap prices by contact count and user count rather than per-seat alone — around $299/mo for 2 users at the Ignite level. Keap looks expensive for one person; Close looks expensive when you add up 10+ reps. The key question is whether you need email marketing automation baked in: Keap includes it, Close doesn't.

## CRM and pipeline management

Both tools have contact management, deal pipelines, and activity logging. Close's pipeline is clean and rep-focused — fast to update, easy to filter by rep or stage, and tightly integrated with calling activity so call outcomes automatically advance the pipeline. Keap's pipeline is designed more for tracking client engagements than managing high-velocity sales: it works well for service businesses with a handful of active proposals but would feel thin for a sales team running 50+ active deals per rep. For pure sales pipeline, Close is the stronger tool.

## Calling and outbound sales

This is Close's defining strength. Close ships a built-in power dialer (click-to-call with automatic logging), a predictive dialer at higher tiers (auto-dials multiple numbers and connects live answers), local presence dialing, voicemail drop, and call recording. For a sales team that competes on call volume, these features replace separate dialer tools like PhoneBurner or Kixie. Keap has basic click-to-call via integration but nothing approaching a purpose-built dialer. If calling is central to your sales motion, Close is categorically better.

## Email and marketing automation

Keap's automation builder is its core strength — multi-step campaigns with conditional branches, contact scoring, automated appointment reminders, follow-up sequences, and broadcast emails to segmented lists. It's the right tool for a small business that needs marketing automation running in the background without a marketing team. Close's email sequences are powerful within a sales context — multi-step, personalized, rep-attributed — but they're built for active prospecting, not passive nurturing. Close doesn't do newsletter sends or automated welcome series. If you need marketing automation, Keap wins.

## Payments and invoicing

Keap includes invoicing and payment collection natively — send an invoice, take payment via Stripe or PayPal, and the CRM records it. For service businesses billing clients directly, this removes the need for a separate invoicing tool. Close has no built-in payments or invoicing; it's purely a sales tool and expects you to handle billing elsewhere (QuickBooks, Stripe, FreshBooks). For product businesses or SaaS, this isn't a gap. For coaches, consultants, or home service companies billing per-project, Keap's payments feature is genuinely valuable.

## Verdict

These tools aren't direct competitors — they serve different sales motions. Close is the right choice for active, rep-led sales teams where call volume and sequence management drive revenue. Keap is the right choice for small-business owners who want a single platform to automate lead capture, client nurturing, and payment collection. If you're asking which is better, the answer is: better for what? A five-rep SaaS sales team should use Close. A solo fitness coach with 200 contacts should use Keap. Rarely does the same business need both.
