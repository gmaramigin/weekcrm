## TL;DR

- **Pick Syncro** if you run a managed service provider business and need PSA and RMM combined — billing, contracts, ticketing, scripting, and remote access in one platform with real MSP-grade depth, at a per-technician price that scales with your team.
- **Pick Spiceworks** if you're an internal IT team or small business on a tight budget that needs a help desk, inventory tracking, and basic network monitoring — and you're comfortable with an ad-supported experience that keeps the price at zero.

## Pricing

The pricing contrast is as stark as it gets in IT tooling. Spiceworks is free — forever, fully functional, ad-supported in the cloud version or self-hosted with no ads. No per-user fees, no seat limits, no upsells for core help desk and network monitoring features. Syncro is a paid platform priced per technician: approximately $139/technician/month (pricing subject to change — verify with Syncro directly). At that rate, a 3-tech MSP is looking at roughly $417/month. The Syncro price includes unlimited endpoints under management, so as your client device count grows, your cost doesn't — which is favorable economics for MSPs. But the baseline investment is incomparable to free. The real question isn't which is cheaper (Spiceworks, obviously) but whether Syncro's MSP-specific capabilities justify the spend for your business model.

## Help desk and ticketing

Both platforms include ticketing, but they're built for different environments. Spiceworks Help Desk handles ticket creation (via email, user portal, or mobile), assignment, basic SLA tracking, and reporting — it's a solid, no-frills help desk for an internal IT team supporting a single organization. The user portal is clean, the email parsing works reliably, and for a one- or two-person IT department, it covers the fundamentals without any configuration overhead. Syncro's ticketing is designed for multi-client MSP environments: ticket creation from monitoring alerts, customer-facing portals, SLA policies per contract type, time tracking that feeds directly into invoicing, and communication templates that look professional when emailing clients. The client-billable time tracking integration alone is a capability Spiceworks doesn't attempt.

## RMM and endpoint management

Syncro includes a full RMM module: agent-based monitoring for Windows and macOS endpoints, alert policies, automated remediation via scripts, patch management for OS and third-party apps, remote access via Splashtop integration, and a device inventory with software and hardware details. Everything an MSP needs to proactively manage client machines without reactive scrambling. Spiceworks has network scanning and basic inventory (SNMP-based network monitoring, IP scanner, device discovery) but no true RMM. It can tell you a device exists and some of its properties; it cannot actively monitor it, push scripts to it, or remotely manage it in a meaningful way. For MSPs, the absence of RMM in Spiceworks isn't a gap — it's a category mismatch.

## Billing and contract management

Syncro's billing module is one of its most distinctive features relative to any free alternative. You define client contracts (managed service agreements, break-fix hourly, block hours, project billing), and Syncro automatically generates invoices from ticket time entries, recurring service fees, and hardware/software sales — then syncs to QuickBooks or Stripe for payment. For an MSP trying to eliminate manual invoicing and ensure every hour gets billed, this integration is operationally significant. Spiceworks has no billing module. It is not designed for businesses that bill clients for IT services — it's designed for IT departments that are cost centers, not revenue generators.

## Network monitoring

Spiceworks has always been strong here for its price point. Its network monitoring tool discovers devices on your network, tracks availability, alerts on outages, and provides a network map view — all for free. For a small business IT team monitoring a single site, it's genuinely competitive with paid alternatives at this scope. Syncro's network monitoring is oriented around MSP use — monitoring client sites remotely through the agent, with alerts that create tickets automatically and feed into the ticketing and billing workflow. It covers the basics but isn't primarily a network monitoring tool the way Spiceworks is.

## Who it's actually for

This comparison hinges on your business model more than any feature. Spiceworks is for internal IT — the solo sysadmin at a 50-person company, the two-person IT team at a school, the help desk at a nonprofit. It handles the job, costs nothing, and has a large community forum that's genuinely useful for troubleshooting. Syncro is for MSPs — businesses that sell IT services to other businesses, need to manage multiple client environments from one pane of glass, and need the billing and time-tracking infrastructure to run a services business profitably. Spiceworks doesn't try to be this; Syncro doesn't try to be free.

## FAQ

**Can Spiceworks handle multiple client organizations for an MSP?**
Spiceworks is not architected for multi-tenant MSP environments. It works within a single organizational context. MSPs who try to use it for client management hit structural limitations quickly.

**Does Syncro have a free trial?**
Syncro offers a trial period — check their website for current terms, as trial length and conditions have varied. There's no permanently free tier.

**Is Spiceworks still actively maintained in 2026?**
Yes. Ziff Davis (owner of Spiceworks) continues to develop the platform. The cloud help desk version receives regular updates; the self-hosted version is less actively developed but still functional.

**Does Syncro include remote access for endpoints?**
Yes. Syncro integrates with Splashtop for remote desktop access to managed endpoints, included in the platform subscription. Technicians can remote in from the ticket or device record directly.

**What does Spiceworks do with the ads it shows?**
Spiceworks displays IT vendor advertisements within the admin interface (not in the user-facing help desk portal). The ads are targeted to IT professionals and are Spiceworks' primary revenue model. Most IT admins find them unobtrusive; if you need an ad-free experience, the self-hosted version eliminates them.

## Verdict

Syncro and Spiceworks serve fundamentally different markets, and choosing between them is less about features and more about your business model. If you run an MSP and need a combined PSA and RMM that handles client billing, multi-tenant endpoint management, and ticketing in one platform, Syncro is purpose-built for that and the per-technician pricing makes economic sense as you scale. If you're an internal IT team or a budget-conscious small business that needs a reliable help desk, inventory tool, and basic network monitoring, Spiceworks delivers genuine value at an unbeatable price. The "competition" between them is almost a category error — pick based on whether you bill clients for IT services, not based on feature counts.
