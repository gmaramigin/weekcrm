## TL;DR

- **Pick Daylite** if you run an Apple-based services business and want a native, offline CRM that tracks client projects alongside client relationships.
- **Pick HubSpot CRM** if you need marketing and sales in one platform, want to start free, and expect to scale.

## Different businesses, not different features

**HubSpot** is built for companies whose growth problem is demand. It combines CRM with email marketing, forms, landing pages, ticketing, and a 1,500+ app marketplace, so a marketing-led SMB can run its entire funnel in one login. The free tier exists precisely to get you in the door before you need any of that.

**Daylite** is built for companies whose growth problem is delivery. A consultancy, law firm, or design agency does not run nurture campaigns — it wins clients through referral and relationship, then manages long engagements. Daylite reflects that by merging **CRM with project management**: the same record holds the client, the deal, and the work.

If you are trying to decide between them, ask which side of that line you fall on. Neither product does the other job well.

## Platform: native Apple vs universal web

Daylite runs only on **macOS, iPhone, and iPad** — no Windows, no Android — and works **fully offline**, syncing when reconnected. It integrates directly with Apple Contacts and Calendar rather than mirroring them. For an all-Mac firm, that produces a CRM that feels like part of the operating system.

HubSpot is a browser product. It runs on any device, on any OS, and never needs installing — which is a strength for mixed hardware and distributed teams, and a non-answer for anyone who wants software that works on a plane.

## Pricing shape

The pricing models diverge in a way that matters more than the headline numbers.

Daylite is **flat: $29/user/mo**, no free tier, no contact tiers, no onboarding fee. Expensive per seat, but you can forecast it three years out.

HubSpot is **stepped**. Free is real and genuinely useful. Starter is **$20/seat/mo**. Then Professional is **$100/seat/mo** with a **$1,500** onboarding charge — a 5x cliff — and Marketing Hub layers contact-based pricing that grows with your database whether or not you add users. Teams routinely get surprised by mid-year bill increases they did not trigger.

For a six-person firm that will never need marketing automation, Daylite costs more and surprises less. For a team that will use the marketing tools, HubSpot's price buys capability Daylite has none of.

## Ecosystem and lock-in

HubSpot's marketplace, API depth, and HubSpot Academy make it easy to extend and easy to hire for — but the flip side is a platform that becomes structurally hard to leave once marketing, ticketing, and sales all live inside it. Daylite's integration library is comparatively narrow and leans on **Zapier** for third-party connections, which is a real limitation if your stack is broad — but it also means the switching cost is low, because Daylite is doing exactly one job.

## Who should pick what

- **Apple-only consultancy managing long client engagements** → Daylite.
- **Marketing-led business generating leads through content** → HubSpot.
- **Team with any Windows or Android devices** → HubSpot.
- **Firm needing offline access on client sites** → Daylite.
- **Startup that wants to begin at zero cost** → HubSpot.
- **Team wanting flat, predictable billing** → Daylite.

## Bottom line

HubSpot is the safer generic recommendation — free to start, universally accessible, and the strongest inbound marketing toolset an SMB can buy — but its cost curve is steep and much of what you pay for is irrelevant to a services firm. Daylite is a narrow, opinionated product that wins decisively for one buyer: the all-Apple small business that manages client projects and needs the CRM to work offline. Decide whether your bottleneck is finding clients or serving them, and the choice makes itself.
