## TL;DR

- **Pick Gleap** if you're a SaaS or product team and your support queue is mostly bug reports and feature requests that need to reach engineers with real technical context.
- **Pick Help Scout** if you're a customer-facing support team that wants a shared inbox that feels like email, with a free tier to start and an AI assistant that quietly handles the routine stuff.

## Two different definitions of "support"

**Gleap** treats support as an input to product development. The product is built around in-app bug reporting: users annotate a screenshot without leaving your app, and the report arrives with console logs and session data attached. Feature request boards and surveys feed a prioritized roadmap view. Live chat and an AI bot exist, but they're in service of the feedback loop. Its natural buyer is a SaaS startup or an agency running client web and mobile apps, where the painful cost is engineering time spent reproducing issues.

**Help Scout** treats support as a relationship. Its founding idea is that good support should feel like a personal email conversation rather than a ticket queue — so the shared inbox looks and behaves like email, replies carry no ticket-number scaffolding, and the whole interface is designed to keep interactions warm at volume. Beacon, its embeddable widget, surfaces knowledge base articles before a customer even opens a chat, cutting ticket volume at the source. Its buyers are SMBs across SaaS, ecommerce, healthcare, and education — often people who found Zendesk too cold and too complex.

Neither framing is wrong. But a team that adopts Help Scout hoping for engineering-grade bug context will be disappointed, and a team that adopts Gleap hoping for a polished general-purpose inbox will find it narrower than expected.

## Pricing shape

**Help Scout** has the friendlier entry: a genuine **free plan for up to 5 users**, then paid from **$25/user/mo**, with the Plus tier at **$45/user/mo** unlocking advanced workflow automation and deeper integrations. **Gleap** starts at a flat **$119/month** on the Team plan, billed annually.

Below about five seats, Help Scout is dramatically cheaper — free, in fact. The crossover comes with headcount: five paid Help Scout users at $25 is $125/mo, already past Gleap's flat rate, and the gap widens on Plus. Gleap's model doesn't punish you for adding people to the inbox; Help Scout's does.

Watch the tier gating on both sides. Help Scout's most useful automation and integrations sit on Plus, so budget against $45 rather than $25 if you need them. Gleap's AI is metered separately at roughly **$0.02 per response** — cheap and predictable, but a line item.

## The capability gap in each direction

Gleap's edge is unrecoverable in Help Scout: **annotated in-app bug capture with console logs**, plus **feature request boards feeding a roadmap**. If your engineers currently waste hours per week on "can you send a screenshot?", nothing in Help Scout closes that.

Help Scout's edge is equally unrecoverable in Gleap: **breadth of general support quality**. Native connectors to Salesforce, Jira, HubSpot, Shopify, and 100+ others on higher tiers; a knowledge base; Beacon's proactive article surfacing; an AI assistant that resolves around **70%** of routine requests. Gleap is explicitly not a CRM and its contact management is minimal, and heavy customization of its widget or portal means working with the SDK.

There's also a fit boundary worth naming: Help Scout is built for customer-facing support and isn't a good ITSM or internal service desk. Gleap isn't either. Neither should be on your list for internal IT.

## Who should pick what

- **SaaS team where bug reproduction is the bottleneck** → Gleap.
- **Ecommerce or services team answering customer questions all day** → Help Scout.
- **Under 5 people, budget of zero** → Help Scout.
- **Growing inbox where per-seat cost compounds** → Gleap.
- **Team that wants feature requests on a public roadmap board** → Gleap.
- **Team that prioritizes tone and a human feel in every reply** → Help Scout.

## Bottom line

Help Scout is the better general help desk — easier to adopt, warmer by design, free to start, and one of the most well-liked tools in the category for good reason. Gleap is the better tool for a specific and common SaaS problem: users report bugs badly, and engineers pay for it. If your inbound skews technical and you want it wired into a roadmap, Gleap's in-app capture justifies its flat $119/mo, especially once your team is big enough that per-seat pricing hurts. If your inbound skews human, Help Scout is the obvious answer and the cheaper one at small scale. Some teams sensibly run Help Scout for customers and Gleap inside the product — just go in knowing that's two subscriptions, not one.
