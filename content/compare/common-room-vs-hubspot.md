## TL;DR

- **Pick HubSpot CRM** if you need the actual go-to-market platform: CRM, marketing automation, service, reporting, and the largest app marketplace in the category.
- **Pick Common Room** if HubSpot is already running and you're losing revenue to intent signals in GitHub, Discord, and LinkedIn that nobody is acting on.

## Platform vs signal layer

**HubSpot** is a full go-to-market suite — sales, marketing automation, customer service, and CMS hubs sharing one contact record. It's the most-adopted CRM among SMB and mid-market companies, with a genuinely usable free tier (unlimited users, up to 1 million contacts) and **1,500+ integrations**. Whatever else your stack contains, HubSpot is likely the thing everything else plugs into.

**Common Room** is one of those things that plugs in. It ingests **50+ signal sources** — GitHub commits, Slack and Discord community activity, LinkedIn engagement, product analytics, job change alerts — uses AI identity resolution to prove that the GitHub handle, LinkedIn profile, and HubSpot contact are one person, scores the account for intent, and syncs it back. Its entire output is *who to talk to, and why now*.

The honest read: these two rarely compete in a real evaluation. What actually happens is a team on HubSpot asks whether Common Room is worth adding.

## The integration story matters more than the feature comparison

If you are going to run both, HubSpot is one of the two CRMs Common Room was clearly built to sit on top of — alongside Salesforce — with **two-way sync** keeping records current in both directions. That's a materially different experience from bolting Common Room onto a CRM it doesn't natively support, where enrichment arrives through middleware and identity mapping becomes your problem.

Practically, that means scored contacts and intent signals land on HubSpot objects your reps are already looking at, inside sequences and workflows they've already built. The intelligence shows up where the work happens instead of in a second tab nobody opens — which is the usual reason intent tools fail to change behaviour.

## Two different pricing traps

HubSpot's is well documented and it's the **price cliff**: Starter at **$20/seat/mo** is cheap, Professional is **$100/seat/mo** plus a one-time **$1,500 onboarding fee**, and Enterprise is **$150/seat/mo**. Marketing Hub then layers contact-tier pricing on top — roughly **$150–$250/mo per additional 5,000 contacts** — which is the mechanism that quietly bumps bills mid-year. Teams leave HubSpot over exactly this, plus paying for modules they never use.

Common Room's trap is structurally similar but differently shaped: the **~$1,000/mo** floor scales with **contact volume**, so success punishes you. A company whose Discord or GitHub audience triples in a year gets a bill that tripled with it, from growth that hasn't yet converted to revenue. Both vendors price on something that grows faster than your headcount; know which variable you're signing up to.

## Scope of use, and who touches it

HubSpot is used by everyone — reps in deals, marketers in campaigns, support in tickets, ops in reporting. That breadth is its strength and its most common complaint: teams that only need a sales CRM find themselves paying for marketing, CMS, and ops surface they'll never open.

Common Room is used by a much smaller group, usually growth or GTM ops plus a handful of reps working an inbound-adjacent motion. That narrowness is fine — but it changes the ROI math. A per-seat platform justifies itself across the whole company; a flat **$1,000/mo** minimum has to justify itself against the incremental pipeline sourced by a few people. That's a real number you can measure, and you should insist on measuring it.

## Who should pick what

- **Need a CRM, marketing automation, or a help desk at all** → HubSpot.
- **Open-source or dev-tools company with an active public community** → Common Room.
- **Marketing-led SMB or mid-market team** → HubSpot.
- **Already on HubSpot with unactioned community and PLG signal** → Common Room.
- **Small team where $1,000/mo is a meaningful share of GTM budget** → HubSpot.
- **Reps who can't tell which of thousands of free users are buying** → Common Room.

## Bottom line

HubSpot is the platform decision and Common Room is the augmentation decision, and they should be made in that order. Almost every go-to-market team needs what HubSpot provides, and its free tier plus **$20/seat** Starter make it easy to start — the discipline required is budgeting for the Professional cliff and contact-tier creep before they arrive. Common Room is a sharp, well-built tool that earns its **$1,000/mo** floor for a specific kind of company: developer-led or PLG, with a real community, already on HubSpot, and demonstrably leaving signal on the table. If that's not you yet, HubSpot alone is the right stack.
