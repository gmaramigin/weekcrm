## TL;DR

- **Pick HubSpot Service Hub** if you want support, sales, and marketing data unified in one CRM — especially if your team already runs on HubSpot.
- **Pick Groove** if you're a small or lean team that needs clean shared-inbox support without CRM overhead or complex onboarding.

## The core difference

HubSpot Service Hub is a helpdesk module built on top of a CRM. Every support interaction lives on the same contact record as sales deals, marketing emails, and website visits. Agents see the full relationship history — not just the current ticket. This is genuinely powerful for B2B teams where support context overlaps with sales context.

Groove is a purpose-built SMB helpdesk. No CRM ambitions, no upsell into a platform ecosystem. The pitch is simplicity: shared inboxes, canned replies, knowledge base, basic automation. It loads fast, new agents learn it in an hour, and the admin burden is minimal.

## Pricing

Groove: plans typically start around $12–16/agent/mo for the base tier, scaling to ~$25–40/agent/mo for plans with more automation, integrations, and reporting. There's usually a free trial. Pricing is transparent and straightforward.

HubSpot Service Hub: Starter at roughly $20/seat/mo is the entry point, but most serious deployments run on Professional (~$100/seat/mo) or Enterprise (~$150/seat/mo). Bundle pricing with Marketing and Sales Hubs can improve the per-product cost, but standalone Service Hub at Professional is a significant step up from Groove.

For a 15-agent team, Groove Professional might cost ~$6k/year versus HubSpot Service Hub Professional at ~$18k/year. The HubSpot premium is worth it only if you're actively using the CRM layer.

## Ticketing and workflow

HubSpot's ticket object is a first-class CRM record — it can have properties, associations to contacts/companies/deals, pipeline stages, and automation triggers that span the full HubSpot platform. Workflow automation at Professional and Enterprise is mature and flexible.

Groove's ticket model is simpler: conversations in shared inboxes, tags, assignees, and rule-based automation for routing and auto-replies. The automation ceiling is lower, but for teams whose complexity is "make sure nothing falls through the cracks," it's sufficient.

## AI and productivity

HubSpot has invested heavily in Breeze, its AI layer, in 2025. Breeze Copilot surfaces in Service Hub as reply drafts, ticket summarization, conversation intelligence, and AI-suggested knowledge articles. The AI pulls from the full CRM context, which makes suggestions more relevant than helpdesk-only AI.

Groove has added AI-assisted reply suggestions and basic summarization. It covers the table stakes of AI-assist but isn't building toward the same CRM-contextual AI that HubSpot is.

## Knowledge base

Both platforms include a knowledge base. HubSpot's is more polished — SEO tools, performance analytics, and the ability to tie article reads to contact records and support ticket deflection metrics.

Groove's knowledge base is clean and easy to set up. Good for teams standing up self-service quickly; less suited for teams running knowledge base as a strategic channel with detailed analytics.

## Omnichannel

HubSpot Service Hub handles email, live chat (via HubSpot Chat), and Facebook Messenger natively, with phone available via integrations (Aircall, JustCall, etc.). The channel list is adequate but narrower than Zendesk or Freshdesk on native integrations.

Groove focuses on email and chat. Social channel support is limited. If omnichannel coverage (WhatsApp, Instagram DM, SMS) is a requirement, both platforms will need third-party connectors — though that need usually signals a different tool altogether.

## Verdict

The decision comes down to whether you're buying a helpdesk or a platform. If support is one slice of a larger HubSpot investment, Service Hub's unified CRM data is a differentiator worth paying for. If you want the simplest helpdesk that a small team can own without a dedicated admin, Groove delivers exactly that at a price that's easy to justify.
