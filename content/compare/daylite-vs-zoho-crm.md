## TL;DR

- **Pick Daylite** if your team is 100% on Mac and iPhone and wants a genuinely native, offline-capable CRM that also handles projects.
- **Pick Zoho CRM** if you need cross-platform access, serious automation, and the cheapest credible per-seat pricing in the category.

## The hardware question comes first

Most CRM comparisons start with features. This one starts with what your team carries. **Daylite is macOS, iPhone, and iPad only** — there is no Windows client and no Android app. That is not an oversight; it is the entire product thesis. Daylite is built to feel like Apple software, syncing with Apple Contacts and Calendar and running as a real desktop application rather than a browser tab.

**Zoho CRM** is a web platform. It runs identically on a ThinkPad, a Chromebook, or a Mac, and its mobile apps are competent on both iOS and Android. For a mixed-device team — which is most teams — Zoho is the only viable option of the two.

So the first filter is binary. If anyone in your company touches Windows, stop evaluating Daylite. If everyone is on Apple hardware and that is a deliberate cultural choice, Daylite becomes interesting in a way no web CRM can match.

## Offline is Daylite's real moat

Daylite stores data locally and syncs when reconnected, so the full app works with no internet at all. For a consultant on a client site, a lawyer in a courthouse, or a field professional between appointments, that is the difference between a CRM they use and one they abandon. Zoho CRM's mobile apps cache some records, but the platform assumes connectivity.

Almost no modern CRM competes here anymore — the category moved to browser-first years ago. Daylite's persistence on native, offline-first architecture is deliberately unfashionable and, for the right buyer, genuinely valuable.

## Depth and price run the other way

Zoho CRM is the deeper product by a wide margin, and it costs less. Plans run **$14/user/mo** (Standard) through **$52/user/mo** (Ultimate) billed annually, with a **free plan for up to 3 users**. That buys workflow automation, custom modules, multi-pipeline management, Zia's AI scoring and forecasting, and Blueprint process enforcement — plus native links into 50+ other Zoho apps.

Daylite starts at **$29/user/mo** and offers no free tier, only a 14-day trial. Its scope is contacts, pipeline, projects, tasks, calendar, and email — well executed, but not a configurable platform. Its integration library is narrow and leans on Zapier for third-party connections, where Zoho ships native connectors.

Put plainly: you pay roughly double for Daylite and get less feature surface. The trade you are buying is native feel and offline reliability.

## CRM plus projects vs CRM plus a suite

Both extend past pure sales, but in opposite directions. **Daylite** bundles **project management into the CRM itself** — you track a client and the work you are doing for them in one place, which fits services firms billing matters, engagements, or cases rather than closing transactional deals.

**Zoho CRM** extends horizontally into the **Zoho ecosystem** — Desk for support, Books for accounting, Campaigns for marketing, Sign for contracts. It is the better answer if you want one vendor across business functions, and Zoho One bundles 45+ apps for **$37/user/mo**.

## Who should pick what

- **Apple-only consultancy or law firm** → Daylite.
- **Any team with Windows or Android users** → Zoho CRM.
- **Reps who work offline or on client sites** → Daylite.
- **Team needing workflow automation and AI scoring** → Zoho CRM.
- **Budget-constrained team of 3 or fewer** → Zoho CRM (free plan).
- **Business managing client projects, not sales pipelines** → Daylite.

## Bottom line

Daylite and Zoho CRM are not really rivals — they are answers to different constraints. Daylite is the best CRM for a small Apple-native services business that values offline reliability and wants client work and client records in the same app, and it charges a premium for that. Zoho CRM is cheaper, far deeper, and runs everywhere, which makes it the default recommendation for anyone whose team is not uniformly on Mac. Check your hardware before you check the feature lists.
