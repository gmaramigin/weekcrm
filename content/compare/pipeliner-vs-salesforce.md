## TL;DR

- **Pick Pipeliner** if you want a visual, sales-centric CRM that reps adopt quickly and that delivers rich reporting and analytics without hiring an admin.
- **Pick Salesforce** if you need a deeply extensible enterprise platform that can model almost any process across sales, service, and marketing — and you can resource the admins, integrations, and budget it requires.

## Pricing

Pipeliner uses straightforward per-user pricing in the mid-tier band, with reporting, customization, and its core features bundled rather than nickel-and-dimed. Salesforce spans a wide range — from accessible Sales Cloud starter tiers to expensive Enterprise and Unlimited editions — but the sticker price understates reality: add-ons, API limits, premium support, and the near-mandatory admin or consulting spend push total cost of ownership well above the license fee. For a sales org that wants predictable cost, Pipeliner is the calmer line item; for an enterprise standardizing on one platform, Salesforce's cost is the price of consolidation.

## Core approach / Data model

Pipeliner is opinionated about being a *sales* CRM. Its data model centers on accounts, contacts, and opportunities rendered through a heavily visual interface — pipelines, org charts, and relationship maps you can read at a glance. The product optimizes for clarity and adoption. Salesforce is a *platform*, not just a CRM: standard and custom objects, a metadata-driven schema, and the ability to build entire applications on top of it. That flexibility means Salesforce can model anything, but you start closer to a blank canvas. Pipeliner gives you a sharp sales tool; Salesforce gives you a foundation you shape.

## Automation and workflows

Salesforce's automation is industrial — Flow, approval processes, and a vast ecosystem let you orchestrate complex multi-object logic across departments. There's almost nothing it can't automate, given the configuration effort. Pipeliner's automation targets the sales workflow specifically: pipeline stage rules, task and activity automation, and notifications that keep reps moving deals forward. It covers the sales motion well and stays approachable. The trade is depth versus accessibility — Salesforce automates the whole enterprise if you invest in it; Pipeliner automates selling without a project plan.

## Email and integrations

Salesforce's AppExchange is the largest CRM marketplace in existence, with thousands of integrations, plus robust APIs and native ties to the broader Salesforce ecosystem (Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Slack). If a tool exists, it probably connects to Salesforce. Pipeliner integrates with the common stack — email sync, Microsoft and Google, and popular business apps — and offers an API, but its ecosystem is far smaller. For a company that needs deep, varied integrations across many systems, Salesforce is unmatched; for a sales team with ordinary connectivity needs, Pipeliner's integrations are sufficient.

## Who should pick what

- **Sales teams wanting fast adoption** and native reporting: **Pipeliner**.
- **Enterprises standardizing on one extensible platform**: **Salesforce**.
- **Organizations without a CRM admin or consulting budget**: **Pipeliner**.
- **Companies needing custom objects, complex automation, or a huge app ecosystem**: **Salesforce**.

## Bottom line

Pipeliner and Salesforce sit at opposite ends of the effort-versus-flexibility spectrum. Pipeliner is a polished, visual sales CRM that delivers value with little setup and no specialist staff — ideal for sales teams that want power without overhead. Salesforce is the enterprise platform you build on when you need to model complex processes and integrate everything, accepting the admins, consultants, and cost that come with it. Match the tool to your appetite for configuration.
