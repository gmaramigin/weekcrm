## TL;DR

- **Pick eWay-CRM** if Outlook is where your team actually works and you want CRM records, project tracking, and communication history to appear inside the inbox rather than in a second app.
- **Pick Zoho CRM** if you want genuine CRM depth — multi-pipeline management, Blueprint process automation, Zia AI, forecasting — at one of the lowest per-seat prices in the category.

## Plugin vs platform

The defining difference isn't features, it's where the software lives. **eWay-CRM** is a Microsoft Outlook plugin. Contacts, deals, and projects render in the same window as email, and every message, note, and call log attaches to the right record automatically because the record is right there. Teams that have failed one or two CRM rollouts often succeed with eWay for exactly one reason: there's nothing new to log into.

**Zoho CRM** is a standalone platform and behaves like one. It expects reps to open it, work pipelines in it, and let managers report out of it. In exchange you get things a plugin structurally can't match — multiple pipelines, custom modules, workflow rules, and analytics built for a sales org rather than a mailbox.

The honest framing: eWay buys you adoption, Zoho buys you capability. Which constraint is binding on your team decides the comparison.

## Pricing models don't line up

These two price on different axes, which makes casual comparison misleading. **eWay-CRM** charges **per team** — there's a free plan with unlimited users for basic contact management, with paid plans starting around **$20/mo** and a Standard plan near **$27/mo**. **Zoho CRM** charges **per user**: free for up to 3 users, then **$14/user/mo** (Standard), **$23** (Professional), **$40** (Enterprise), and **$52** (Ultimate) billed annually.

For a five-person Outlook shop, eWay's team pricing is hard to beat on raw cost. But note where Zoho's value sits: the automation and Zia AI that justify the comparison at all are locked to Enterprise ($40) and above. Compare eWay's Standard against Zoho Enterprise, not Zoho Standard, if AI and Blueprint are what you're after.

## Ecosystem gravity

Both products are bets on an ecosystem, just different ones. eWay-CRM is a **Microsoft** bet — Outlook, Microsoft 365, Teams for shared threads and discussion, plus 3,000+ integrations through Zapier and friends for QuickBooks, Mailchimp, and Adobe Sign. Its value compounds the more Microsoft-centric you are, and evaporates entirely if you aren't.

Zoho CRM is a **Zoho** bet — 50+ apps including Desk, Books, Campaigns, and Sign, natively integrated, with Zoho One bundling the whole suite at roughly **$37/user/mo**. That's real consolidation value if you're willing to move more of the business onto Zoho, and a mild lock-in concern if you're not.

## Where each one is weak

eWay's own weak spots are pipeline visualization and reporting — a plugin's UI constraints show up exactly where a sales manager wants a clean funnel view — and its modular per-team pricing is genuinely confusing to evaluate. Zoho's weak spots are setup complexity from sheer configuration surface, a UI that still feels dated next to newer CRMs, and the tier-gating that pushes real automation to $40/user/mo. Neither product hides these; they're the predictable cost of each design choice.

## Who should pick what

- **Outlook-and-Teams shop that's failed a CRM rollout before** → eWay-CRM.
- **Sales team needing multiple pipelines and forecasting** → Zoho CRM.
- **Consultancy wanting CRM plus project tracking in the inbox** → eWay-CRM.
- **Team already using Zoho Books, Desk, or Campaigns** → Zoho CRM.
- **Small team optimizing for total cost, not per-seat cost** → eWay-CRM.
- **Team that wants AI lead scoring and process enforcement** → Zoho CRM.

## Bottom line

eWay-CRM and Zoho CRM are both value plays, but they solve opposite failure modes. eWay wins when the risk is that reps won't use the CRM — putting it inside Outlook removes the excuse, and the team-based pricing is unusually kind to small firms. Zoho wins when the risk is that the CRM won't do enough — Blueprint, Zia, and multi-pipeline reporting are things no Outlook plugin will match, at prices Salesforce can't touch. Diagnose your bottleneck first: adoption or capability.
