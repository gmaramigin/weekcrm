## TL;DR

- **Pick Issuetrak** if tickets arrive from employees or business customers and you need configurable forms, routing rules, SLA enforcement, or on-premises hosting.
- **Pick Tidio** if you sell to consumers online and your support problem is answering website visitors fast, at volume, without hiring.

## Ticketing versus conversation

**Issuetrak** is a help desk in the classic sense. Over 20 years it has been sharpened around structured intake — custom issue forms, user-defined fields, templates, round-robin assignment, and trigger-based automation for escalations and SLA tracking. Requests come in by email, web portal, chat, or mobile and become records that must be routed, worked, and closed with an audit trail. This is the shape of internal IT, facilities, HR, and B2B support.

**Tidio** is a chat-first product. Its primitives are the widget, the visitor, and the Flow — no-code sequences that capture leads, offer discounts, and answer questions. Its AI agent, **Lyro**, handles a claimed **up to 67% of inquiries automatically**, mostly product details and order status. Requests arrive from anonymous browsers who will leave in thirty seconds if nobody answers.

The distinction matters more than any feature list: one product optimizes for handling work correctly, the other for handling it immediately.

## B2B back office vs B2C storefront

Tidio is explicit about its center of gravity — ecommerce stores, with deep native **Shopify and WooCommerce** integrations and a multi-channel inbox spanning email, live chat, Messenger, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Those are consumer channels. Tidio's own weakness, by its own account, is B2B account management.

Issuetrak sits precisely where Tidio is weak: manufacturing, healthcare, government, and internal operations, where the requester is a known user with an account, a priority, and an SLA. It has no ecommerce hooks, no proactive visitor targeting, and no social channels. Trying to run a Shopify support desk on Issuetrak would be as awkward as running a hospital IT desk on Tidio.

## Deployment and data control

This is the cleanest disqualifier. Issuetrak offers a real **on-premises option** alongside cloud — increasingly rare, and the reason it survives in regulated environments where newer SaaS tools cannot bid. Tidio is cloud-only. If your security policy requires data on your own infrastructure, the evaluation stops here and Tidio is out regardless of how good Lyro is.

## Pricing shape

Issuetrak charges **from $27/agent/mo with every feature available at every tier** — no paywalled capabilities, no expanding tiers. Budgeting is a headcount multiplication.

Tidio starts **free** with usable live chat and basic ticketing, and paid plans begin at **$24.17/mo**. The catch is layering: **Lyro AI and advanced Flows are priced as add-ons**, so an AI-heavy deployment costs considerably more than the entry number suggests, and the **Plus plan starts around $749/mo** — a large step for teams that need proper team features. Tidio is cheaper to start and less predictable to scale; Issuetrak is the reverse.

## Who should pick what

- **Internal IT or operations desk with SLAs** → Issuetrak.
- **Shopify or WooCommerce store answering pre-sale questions** → Tidio.
- **Organization requiring on-premises data residency** → Issuetrak.
- **Lean team wanting AI to absorb repetitive consumer queries** → Tidio.
- **Buyer who wants all features included with no add-on ladder** → Issuetrak.
- **Business supporting customers on Instagram, Messenger, and WhatsApp** → Tidio.

## Bottom line

These tools solve support problems at opposite ends of a business. Issuetrak is a workmanlike, highly configurable ticketing system whose real selling points are on-premises deployment and flat all-inclusive pricing — the interface is dated and it says so. Tidio is one of the most accessible AI chat tools going, with a genuinely useful free plan and an ecommerce integration story Issuetrak cannot match, as long as you watch the add-on costs. Pick by who is filing the request: an employee with an account, or a shopper with a cart.
