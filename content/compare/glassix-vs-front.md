## TL;DR

- **Pick Glassix** if you're running high contact volume and want AI agents to resolve, escalate, and follow up without a human in the loop for qualified inquiries — voice included.
- **Pick Front** if your customer requests cross department lines and the hard problem is keeping context intact through handoffs between sales, ops, and support.

## AI-autonomous vs human-collaborative

The two products start from opposite premises about who should handle a conversation. **Glassix** is built on the claim that AI should *run* the contact center rather than assist it. Its agents take inquiries end to end — resolving simple ones, escalating what they can't, following up afterward — across messaging channels and voice via CrystalVoice, with multilingual handling that doesn't require separate configurations per language.

**Front** starts from the agent. It's a shared inbox that happens to have full ticketing, routing, and SLA tracking underneath, and its signature capability is cross-team workflow: routing and escalating without losing conversation context or forcing a cold handoff. Front's AI Autopilot can absorb up to 70% of inbound on qualified plans, but the product's identity is still a workspace humans work in together.

That difference determines everything else — pricing structure, rollout effort, and what a failed implementation looks like.

## Which one fits your inquiry mix

The deciding question isn't "how much support volume do you have," it's "how much of it is answerable." Glassix's economics work when a large share of contacts are resolvable from policy and knowledge content — order status, booking changes, account questions — which is why it targets e-commerce, hospitality, automotive, higher education, insurance, and real estate.

Front's economics work when contacts are *not* independently resolvable. Its 9,000+ customers skew logistics, financial services, and professional services, where a single request might legitimately require a salesperson, a fulfillment coordinator, and a support agent in sequence. No autonomous agent resolves that; the win is making the handoff invisible to the customer.

Audit a week of your own tickets before choosing. The answer usually falls out of the data.

## Pricing and where AI actually costs

**Front** is cheaper to enter at **$25/user/mo** (Starter), **$65** (Professional), **$105** (Enterprise) — but Starter caps at 10 seats and one channel, which makes it a trial tier more than a plan, and AI Autopilot and Copilot are paid add-ons on lower tiers.

**Glassix** starts at **$49/user/mo** (Starter) with Growth at **$65** and custom Enterprise pricing, and offers a **30-day free trial** — unusually long, which matters for a product whose value depends on how well you can train it. Its own caveat mirrors Front's: advanced AI capability is an add-on on Growth, not included in base pricing.

Net: at the tier where either product is actually deployable with AI, both land near **$65/user/mo** before add-ons. Don't decide on the entry price.

## Implementation risk

Glassix carries more of it. AI-first means your outcomes depend on configuration quality and the knowledge content you feed it — a thin rollout produces an agent that deflects without resolving, which is worse than no AI. It's also a newer platform with a smaller integration ecosystem than incumbents like Zendesk.

Front's risk is different and milder: you get a working shared inbox immediately, and the failure mode is paying for workflow sophistication a simpler team doesn't need. Front itself notes it can be overkill for straightforward support operations. Its **160+ integrations** mean CRM and ERP connections rarely require custom work.

## Who should pick what

- **High-volume B2C support with repetitive inquiries** → Glassix.
- **Requests that route across sales, ops, and support** → Front.
- **Voice is a primary channel you want AI to answer** → Glassix.
- **Team wants an email-like experience agents already understand** → Front.
- **Multilingual support without per-language setup** → Glassix.
- **You need deep CRM and ERP integrations out of the box** → Front.

## Bottom line

Glassix and Front are both credible AI-era customer engagement platforms, but they're not interchangeable. Glassix is the aggressive bet — commit to AI resolution, invest in configuration, and stop scaling headcount with volume. Front is the durable bet — give a human team a workspace where conversations survive every handoff, then add AI where it earns its keep. Pick Glassix if most of your inquiries could be answered by a well-briefed machine; pick Front if they genuinely couldn't.
