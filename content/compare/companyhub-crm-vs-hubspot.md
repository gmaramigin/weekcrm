## TL;DR

- **Pick CompanyHub CRM** if you're a sales-focused SMB with an unusual process, you want to model it yourself without code, and you want a bill that doesn't cliff.
- **Pick HubSpot CRM** if you want marketing automation, a free tier to start on, and the safety of the most widely adopted CRM in the SMB and mid-market.

## Focused customization vs the default platform

**CompanyHub** has a clear pitch: Salesforce-style flexibility without the Salesforce project. Custom fields, custom tables, custom relationships, and custom reporting — all configurable by an ops-minded salesperson rather than an admin or a consultant. Around that sits solid sales machinery: two-way Gmail and Outlook sync with tracking and templates, bulk email and drip sequences from inside the CRM, and reporting you can shape yourself. It is a sales tool and it does not pretend to be more.

**HubSpot** is the opposite bet. It's a suite — sales, marketing automation, service, CMS — sharing one contact record, backed by **1,500+ integrations** and HubSpot Academy's enormous body of training material. Its advantage isn't any single feature; it's that everything already connects to it and everyone already knows it.

The real question is whether your process is unusual enough that shaping it yourself beats adopting the industry default. Most teams' processes are not that unusual. Some genuinely are, and those teams are underserved by HubSpot's opinionated defaults.

## Cost predictability is CompanyHub's strongest argument

Entry prices look nearly identical — **$21/user/mo** for CompanyHub, **$20/seat/mo** for HubSpot Starter — and that similarity is misleading. CompanyHub runs three plans topping out at **$64/user/mo**, and that's the whole surface area of the bill.

HubSpot's shape is very different. Professional is **$100/seat/mo** with a one-time **$1,500 onboarding fee**, Enterprise is **$150/seat/mo**, and Marketing Hub prices on contact tiers at roughly **$150–$250/mo per additional 5,000 contacts**. That contact-tier mechanism is what generates mid-year bill surprises, and the **5x jump from Starter to Professional** is the single most cited reason teams leave. It's not a hidden cost — it's published — but plenty of buyers only model year one.

For a ten-person sales team, CompanyHub's ceiling is around **$640/mo**. Ten HubSpot Professional seats are **$1,000/mo** before marketing contacts, plus onboarding. If budget predictability over three years matters more than ecosystem breadth, CompanyHub wins that argument cleanly.

## Ecosystem and hiring risk cut the other way

CompanyHub's weaknesses are structural rather than functional. The integrations library is **substantially smaller**, so the odds that your niche billing tool, data warehouse, or sales engagement platform has a native connector are lower — expect Zapier or API work. The mobile app is functional but noticeably behind the desktop experience. And **brand recognition is low**, which sounds cosmetic until it isn't: procurement pushes back on unknown vendors, there's no bench of consultants to call, no library of tutorials, and no candidates whose résumé already says they know it.

HubSpot's ubiquity is genuinely load-bearing here. You can hire someone who already knows it, find an agency in any city, and assume almost any SaaS tool you buy has a HubSpot integration on its landing page. That's worth real money, even though it's not a feature you can demo.

## Scope: what you're paying for that you'll never use

The clearest way to separate these is to count what's outside your use case. HubSpot's breadth means a sales-only team pays for marketing, CMS, and ops surface they never open — that feature bloat is a documented reason teams churn out. CompanyHub has almost no such surface; every module in it is for selling.

Flip it and the logic reverses. If you *do* need email campaigns, landing pages, forms, and a knowledge base, HubSpot's unified contact record across all of it is a genuine advantage that CompanyHub cannot approach — you'd be buying and integrating three or four more tools.

## Who should pick what

- **Sales-only team with a non-standard process** → CompanyHub.
- **Need marketing automation, landing pages, or a help desk** → HubSpot.
- **Want a bill you can forecast for three years** → CompanyHub.
- **Want to start at $0 and prove the need first** → HubSpot.
- **Larger org where vendor recognition affects procurement** → HubSpot.
- **Value clean email automation and no-code custom objects over ecosystem size** → CompanyHub.

## Bottom line

HubSpot is the safe default and deserves to be — the free tier is genuinely usable, the ecosystem is unmatched, and you'll never have to defend the choice. The cost is the Starter-to-Professional cliff, contact-tier creep, and paying for modules a sales team won't touch. CompanyHub is the deliberate alternative: cheaper at the top end, flexible enough to model an unusual process without a developer, and free of the suite you didn't want — at the price of a thinner integrations library and a name nobody recognizes. If your sales process is ordinary and you want the ecosystem, take HubSpot. If it's unusual and you want predictable spend on a pure sales tool, trial CompanyHub.
