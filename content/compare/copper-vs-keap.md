## TL;DR

- **Pick Copper** if your business is relationship-driven, your team uses Gmail daily, and you want a CRM that fills itself in from email threads with zero data entry. Built for agencies, consultancies, and professional services.
- **Pick Keap** if you're a small-business owner or solopreneur who needs automated lead capture, email nurturing, appointment booking, and payment collection from one platform — without hiring a marketing or ops person.

## Pricing

Copper is per-seat with tiered pricing: around $9/user/mo at Starter (limited to 3 users and basic features), $49/user/mo at Professional, and $99/user/mo at Business. Most growing teams sit on Professional. Keap prices differently — around $299/mo for up to 2 users at the base tier, with higher plans supporting more users and automation volume. For a 2-person team, Copper Professional is $98/mo and Keap is $299/mo. But Keap's bill replaces separate spending on email marketing (Mailchimp runs $50–$100/mo at small-list sizes) and potentially invoicing tools — so the net cost comparison is closer than it looks.

## Google Workspace integration

Copper's defining feature is its Gmail sidebar — visible inside every email thread, it shows the contact's CRM record, open deals, recent activity, and lets you log notes or create tasks without leaving Gmail. Contacts get auto-created when you email someone new; meetings log against contacts from Google Calendar automatically. For a team that handles most client communication in Gmail, this eliminates the classic CRM complaint: "I have to update two systems." Keap has no equivalent Google Workspace integration. If your team lives in Gmail, this gap is decisive.

## Marketing and email automation

This is Keap's home turf. The Keap automation builder lets you chain triggers and actions across the client lifecycle — new lead signs up via web form, gets a welcome email, is added to a nurture sequence, receives a booking link after sequence completion, and gets an invoice auto-sent after the appointment is marked done. Each step is configurable and runs without manual intervention. Copper has basic workflow automation at the Professional tier (stage-change notifications, task creation) but nothing approaching Keap's full marketing automation engine. For automated client communication at scale, Keap is the stronger platform.

## Pipeline and deal tracking

Both tools include pipeline management. Copper's pipeline is visual and frictionless — drag deals through stages, filter by owner or stage, see activity history inline. Because Copper auto-logs emails and meetings, the pipeline stays current without manual updates, which is genuinely useful for professional services teams running long sales cycles with irregular touch patterns. Keap's pipeline tracks lead and client stages well for simpler flows but lacks the depth and filtering Copper offers. For serious pipeline management, Copper is the better tool.

## Payments and billing

Keap wins here outright. Built-in invoicing, payment collection via Stripe and PayPal, recurring subscriptions, and quote-to-invoice workflows are all native to Keap. For a service business that sends 20–50 invoices a month, having invoicing inside the same tool as CRM and email is a genuine operational simplification. Copper has no billing functionality — you'd integrate with QuickBooks, FreshBooks, or Stripe separately. For product-led or SaaS businesses, that's fine. For service businesses billing hourly or per-project, Keap's payments feature is a practical differentiator.

## Verdict

Copper and Keap are each well-designed but for completely different customers. Copper is the right choice for professional services teams that communicate primarily through Gmail and want a CRM that stays current without manual data entry — agencies, consultants, recruiters. Keap is the right choice for owner-operated service businesses — coaches, therapists, home service providers — who need automated lead-to-payment workflows without hiring a dedicated marketing or operations person. The question to ask: is your bottleneck relationship visibility and pipeline clarity (Copper), or lead nurturing and client billing automation (Keap)?
